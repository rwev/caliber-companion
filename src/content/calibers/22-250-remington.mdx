## History

The .22-250 Remington has one of the longest wildcat pedigrees of any factory cartridge. Its origins trace back to around 1937, when experimenters like J.E. Gebby and others began necking down the .250 Savage case to accept .224-caliber bullets, creating what was informally known as the .22 Varminter. The wildcat quickly earned a reputation for spectacular velocity and flat trajectory, making it devastatingly effective on prairie dogs, woodchucks, and coyotes at distances that challenged the limits of the popular .220 Swift. Throughout the 1940s and 1950s, the .22 Varminter grew in popularity among handloaders, with several gunsmiths and wildcatters producing slightly different versions.

Remington formally standardized the cartridge in 1965 under the .22-250 Remington designation, introducing it in their Model 700 bolt-action rifle. The factory legitimization was largely a formality — the cartridge had already proven itself over nearly three decades of wildcat use — but it opened the door to factory ammunition production and wider commercial availability. The .22-250 quickly overtook the .220 Swift in popularity, aided by its reputation for better barrel life and its compatibility with the widely available .308-family bolt face.

## Modern Usage

The .22-250 Remington remains the standard by which high-velocity varmint cartridges are judged. Pushing 50-grain bullets beyond 4,000 fps, it delivers remarkably flat trajectories and explosive terminal performance on small-bodied animals, making it the preferred choice for serious prairie dog shooters and predator hunters across the American West. The cartridge's combination of raw speed and excellent accuracy in heavy-barreled varmint rifles has earned it a loyal following that newer cartridges have been unable to dislodge. While the .204 Ruger offers even higher velocity with lighter bullets and the .223 Remington provides a cheaper, lower-recoil alternative, the .22-250 occupies a sweet spot of velocity, energy, and wind-bucking ability that keeps it relevant. Its one limitation is barrel life — the high velocities come at the cost of increased throat erosion — but for hunters who value downrange performance over round count, the .22-250 remains hard to beat.
