## History

The .460 Smith & Wesson Magnum was introduced in January 2005, just two years after the .500 S&W Magnum. Where the .500 pursued maximum bore size and energy, the .460 was designed around velocity and versatility. Developed by Hornady and Smith & Wesson, the cartridge is based on a lengthened .454 Casull case, which itself descends from the .45 Colt. This lineage gives the .460 S&W a unique advantage: any revolver chambered for it can also fire .454 Casull and .45 Colt ammunition.

Smith & Wesson introduced the cartridge in the Model 460XVR (Xtreme Velocity Revolver), built on the same X-Frame platform as the Model 500. With a 200-grain bullet, the .460 S&W achieves velocities exceeding 2,300 fps from an 8.375-inch barrel — faster than many rifle cartridges and producing remarkably flat trajectories for a revolver round. The combination of extreme velocity and the ability to shoot three different cartridges made it one of the most versatile revolvers ever produced.

## Modern Usage

The .460 S&W Magnum is primarily a handgun hunting cartridge, and a remarkably capable one. Its flat trajectory allows confident shots at distances that would be impractical with slower magnum revolver cartridges. With heavier 300-grain bullets, it delivers the deep penetration needed for large and dangerous game, while lighter 200-grain loads achieve velocities that create devastating terminal effect on deer and similar game.

The three-cartridge compatibility is a genuine practical advantage. Shooters can practice with mild .45 Colt loads, step up to .454 Casull for moderate-power applications, and load .460 S&W Magnum when maximum performance is needed. This makes the platform more practical than the .500 S&W for shooters who want a versatile big-bore revolver rather than a single-purpose powerhouse. Ammunition availability remains limited compared to mainstream cartridges, and the revolver's size and recoil restrict its appeal to dedicated handgun hunters and big-bore enthusiasts.
