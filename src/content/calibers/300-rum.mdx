## History

The .300 Remington Ultra Magnum was introduced by Remington in 1999 as the flagship cartridge in their Ultra Magnum family. Based on the massive, unbelted .404 Jeffery case necked down to .30 caliber, the .300 RUM offered a dramatic increase in case capacity and velocity over the .300 Winchester Magnum. Where the .300 Win Mag typically pushes 180-grain bullets at 2,960 fps, the .300 RUM achieves 3,250 fps with the same bullet weight — a velocity advantage of nearly 300 fps that translates to significantly flatter trajectory and greater retained energy at extreme distances.

The .300 RUM generated considerable excitement upon its introduction, particularly among long-range hunters and the U.S. military. The Army evaluated it for the M24A3 extended-range sniper system, recognizing its potential to deliver effective fire at distances beyond the capabilities of the standard 7.62 NATO. However, the military ultimately moved toward the .300 Win Mag and .338 Lapua Magnum for most applications. In the civilian market, the .300 RUM developed a devoted following among Western hunters who pursued elk and mule deer across vast expanses where shots at 500 yards and beyond were realistic possibilities.

## Modern Usage

The .300 Remington Ultra Magnum occupies the extreme end of the .30-caliber performance spectrum. Its enormous case capacity allows it to push 200 to 210-grain high-BC bullets at velocities that exceed the .300 Win Mag by 150 to 250 fps, providing meaningfully less drop and wind drift at ranges beyond 500 yards. For hunters who regularly take shots at extreme distances in open terrain, this advantage is tangible and can be the difference between a clean hit and a miss.

The trade-offs, however, are significant. The .300 RUM produces approximately 32 ft-lbs of free recoil energy — punishing even in a heavy rifle — and barrel life is typically limited to 800 to 1,200 rounds due to the extreme powder charges and resulting throat erosion. Ammunition availability has also been a persistent challenge, as the cartridge never achieved the widespread commercial success of the .300 Win Mag. Remington's bankruptcy and subsequent restructuring further complicated the supply situation. Despite these limitations, the .300 RUM retains a loyal niche following among hunters and long-range shooters who value its raw ballistic performance. For those willing to manage the recoil and invest in handloading, it remains one of the most capable .30-caliber cartridges ever designed.
