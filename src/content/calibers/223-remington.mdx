## History

The .223 Remington was born from the U.S. military's search for a small-caliber, high-velocity cartridge to replace the 7.62×51mm NATO in infantry rifles. In the late 1950s, Remington Arms partnered with ArmaLite engineer Eugene Stoner to develop a .22-caliber cartridge suitable for the AR-15 rifle platform. Working from Robert Hutton's earlier .222 Remington Special wildcat, Remington lengthened the .222 Remington case slightly to achieve the velocity and terminal performance the military required. The resulting cartridge was commercially introduced as the .223 Remington in 1964, the same year the military variant was standardized as the 5.56×45mm NATO and the M16 rifle began fielding to U.S. troops in Vietnam.

While the .223 Remington and 5.56 NATO share identical external dimensions, they are not perfectly interchangeable. The 5.56 NATO is loaded to higher chamber pressures and features a longer-throated chamber (known as the NATO or mil-spec chamber), while the .223 Remington adheres to SAAMI specifications with a shorter throat and slightly lower maximum pressure. Firing 5.56 NATO ammunition in a rifle chambered exclusively for .223 Remington can produce excessive pressures, though the reverse — firing .223 in a 5.56 chamber — is considered safe. Many modern rifles are chambered in .223 Wylde, a hybrid chamber designed to safely fire both cartridges with optimal accuracy.

## Modern Usage

Today the .223 Remington is the most popular centerfire rifle cartridge in the United States by a wide margin, driven almost entirely by the dominance of the AR-15 platform. Tens of millions of AR-pattern rifles have been sold to civilians, and the .223/5.56 is their default chambering. The cartridge's very low recoil — roughly 3.5 ft-lbs in a typical AR-15 — makes it accessible to virtually all shooters regardless of size or experience, and ammunition is among the least expensive centerfire rifle options available.

In the field, the .223 Remington excels as a varmint and predator cartridge, with lightweight bullets in the 40-55 grain range delivering explosive fragmentation on prairie dogs, groundhogs, and coyotes. Heavier 62-77 grain match bullets have made the cartridge competitive in practical rifle matches and service rifle competitions out to 600 yards. Law enforcement agencies across the United States have adopted AR-15 pattern rifles in .223/5.56 as standard patrol rifles, replacing shotguns for their superior accuracy, reduced overpenetration risk with proper ammunition selection, and effective range. While the cartridge is legal for deer hunting in many states, most experienced hunters consider it marginal for game larger than coyotes and recommend heavier calibers for ethical harvests on deer-sized animals.
