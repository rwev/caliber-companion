## History

The 7×57mm Mauser was developed by Paul Mauser in 1892 for the Spanish military's Model 1893 rifle, making it one of the first military cartridges designed around smokeless powder and a modern rimless, bottleneck case. It received its baptism of fire during the Spanish-American War of 1898, where Spanish defenders at the Battle of San Juan Hill used their 7mm Mausers to devastating effect against American troops armed with .30-40 Krag rifles and single-shot .45-70 Trapdoor Springfields. The casualties inflicted on the American forces that day directly led the U.S. to adopt the Mauser action design and develop what would become the .30-06 Springfield.

The cartridge earned further combat distinction during the Second Boer War (1899-1902), where Boer marksmen used 7mm Mauser rifles to inflict punishing losses on British forces at ranges that left the defenders helpless. The flat trajectory and moderate recoil of the 7×57mm made it extraordinarily effective in trained hands. Throughout the early 20th century, it was adopted by numerous nations across Latin America, the Middle East, and Africa.

## Modern Usage

The 7×57mm Mauser has transitioned gracefully from military service to sporting use, earning admiration from some of the most respected figures in hunting history. W.D.M. "Karamojo" Bell famously used the 7×57mm to take over 1,000 elephants in Africa, demonstrating the cartridge's remarkable sectional density and penetration with heavy-for-caliber bullets. Today it remains a favorite among discerning hunters, particularly in Europe and Africa, for medium to large game. Known as the .275 Rigby in British sporting tradition, it offers a superb balance of flat trajectory, moderate recoil, and excellent terminal performance. Commercial ammunition is available from manufacturers like Hornady, PPU, and Norma, and the cartridge continues to be chambered in quality sporting rifles from makers like CZ and Ruger.
