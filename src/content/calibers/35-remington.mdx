## History

The .35 Remington was introduced in 1906 as part of a family of rimless cartridges designed for Remington's new Model 8 semi-automatic rifle — one of the first commercially successful autoloading sporting rifles. The cartridge family included the .25, .30, .32, and .35 Remington, all designed by the prolific firearms engineer John Browning. Of the four, the .35 Remington proved the most enduring, offering a heavy .358-caliber bullet at moderate velocity that delivered devastating close-range terminal performance on deer and black bear.

While the Remington Model 8 and its successor, the Model 81, gave the .35 Remington its start, the cartridge truly found its home in the Marlin Model 336 lever-action rifle beginning in 1948. The combination of the fast-handling Marlin lever gun and the hard-hitting .35 Remington became a favorite in the thick timber and dense brush of the Northeast, Upper Midwest, and Southeast, where deer and bear are often encountered at ranges inside 100 yards. The cartridge carved out a niche as a heavier-hitting alternative to the .30-30 Winchester, offering significantly more energy from a larger-diameter bullet while fitting in the same compact lever-action platform.

## Modern Usage

The .35 Remington occupies a niche but dedicated position in the American hunting landscape. Its heavy 200-grain bullets at around 2,100 fps deliver authoritative terminal performance on deer and black bear at woods ranges, and the large frontal area creates wide wound channels that result in short tracking jobs. Hornady's LEVERevolution ammunition breathed new life into the cartridge by introducing a pointed FTX bullet safe for tubular magazines, improving both velocity and effective range compared to traditional flat-nosed or round-nosed loads. The .35 Remington remains particularly popular in states with thick cover and short sight lines — Maine, Michigan, Wisconsin, and the Appalachian states — where its rainbow trajectory beyond 200 yards is irrelevant and its close-range authority is a genuine advantage. Ammunition availability has tightened in recent years, and the cartridge's future depends largely on the continued production of lever-action rifles chambered for it. Nevertheless, for hunters who understand its strengths and limitations, the .35 Remington remains one of the most effective close-range deer and bear cartridges available.
