## History

The .204 Ruger was jointly developed by Hornady and Ruger, introduced in 2004 as the first commercially produced .20-caliber centerfire cartridge. Based on the .222 Remington Magnum case necked down to accept .204-inch diameter bullets, the cartridge was designed from the ground up for extreme velocity varmint shooting. At its introduction, the 32-grain loading achieved 4,225 fps, making it the fastest commercially produced rifle cartridge in the world. The development team chose the .20 caliber specifically to optimize the balance between velocity, wind drift, and barrel life.

The cartridge arrived during a period of intense interest in small-caliber, high-velocity varmint rounds, competing with established cartridges like the .22-250 Remington and .220 Swift. Ruger chambered several bolt-action models, and Hornady provided the ammunition. The .204 Ruger quickly attracted a following among dedicated prairie dog and ground squirrel shooters who appreciated its remarkably flat trajectory and reduced wind drift compared to the .22 centerfires at moderate ranges. Multiple manufacturers adopted the chambering, though it never achieved the widespread commercial penetration of the .223 Remington.

## Modern Usage

The .204 Ruger has settled into a well-defined niche as a specialized varmint and predator cartridge, prized by serious small-game hunters and pest control shooters. Its extreme velocity produces nearly instant fragmentation of lightweight bullets on impact, making it devastatingly effective on prairie dogs, ground squirrels, and woodchucks while minimizing the risk of ricochet. For predator hunters pursuing coyotes, the 40-grain bullet loadings offer better penetration while still delivering impressively flat trajectories out to 300 yards and beyond.

The cartridge's greatest practical advantage is its minimal recoil, which allows shooters to spot their own impacts through the scope and make rapid follow-up shots during high-volume shooting sessions. Barrel life is respectable at approximately 3,000 to 4,000 rounds, better than the .22-250 and .220 Swift despite similar velocities. Factory ammunition is available from Hornady, Federal, and several other manufacturers, though handloaders can optimize the cartridge further with the growing selection of .20-caliber bullets. While it remains a niche round, the .204 Ruger has earned lasting respect as one of the finest purpose-built varmint cartridges ever produced.
