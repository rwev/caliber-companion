## History

The 5.7×28mm cartridge was developed by the Belgian firm FN Herstal beginning in 1986 as part of a NATO requirement for a new personal defense weapon (PDW) cartridge that could defeat standardized body armor at 200 meters. NATO sought a replacement for the 9×19mm Parabellum in the PDW role, recognizing that modern body armor had rendered pistol-caliber submachine guns increasingly ineffective for rear-echelon troops who needed compact, lightweight defensive weapons. FN's solution was a tiny bottlenecked cartridge firing a .224-caliber projectile at rifle-like velocities from an incredibly compact package — the 5.7×28mm, paired with the revolutionary P90 submachine gun introduced in 1990.

The P90 and its cartridge represented a radical departure from conventional thinking. The bullpup P90 held 50 rounds in a horizontal magazine that sat atop the receiver, and the 5.7×28mm's armor-piercing SS190 projectile could defeat NATO CRISAT body armor (titanium and Kevlar) at 200 meters. FN later developed the Five-seveN pistol chambered in the same cartridge, creating a PDW-pistol system sharing common ammunition. The cartridge competed against Heckler & Koch's 4.6×30mm for NATO adoption, and in 2003, NATO selected the 5.7×28mm as the recommended PDW cartridge under STANAG 4509, though adoption by member nations has been gradual. The armor-piercing SS190 variant remains restricted to military and law enforcement, while commercial sporting ammunition uses conventional lead-core or polymer-tipped bullets.

## Modern Usage

The 5.7×28mm has experienced a significant surge in popularity on the civilian market in recent years. For decades it was essentially a two-gun cartridge — available only in the FN Five-seveN pistol and the PS90 semi-automatic carbine (the civilian P90 variant). That changed when Ruger introduced the Ruger-57 pistol in 2020 at a substantially lower price point than the Five-seveN, followed by CMMG offering AR-pattern firearms chambered in the cartridge. These new platforms brought the 5.7×28mm to a much broader audience and drove ammunition prices down through increased demand and production volume.

The cartridge's appeal to civilian shooters centers on its remarkably low recoil — approximately 2 ft-lbs in the Five-seveN, comparable to a .22 LR in a heavy pistol — combined with high velocity and flat trajectory relative to conventional handgun cartridges. The Five-seveN's 20-round flush-fit magazine capacity also significantly exceeds typical 9mm pistols. Critics note that the cartridge's small, lightweight projectiles produce substantially less muzzle energy than 9mm Parabellum, raising questions about terminal effectiveness with commercial (non-armor-piercing) ammunition. Proponents counter that the high velocity and fragmentation characteristics of loads like the SS197SR 40-grain V-MAX compensate for the lower mass. In military and law enforcement contexts, the 5.7×28mm continues to serve in its intended PDW role, notably with the U.S. Secret Service which adopted the P90 for executive protection details where its compact size, armor-defeating capability, and controllability in close-quarters situations are particularly valued.
