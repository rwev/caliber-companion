## History

The .45 Colt was developed in 1872 by a partnership between Colt's Patent Firearms Manufacturing Company and the Union Metallic Cartridge Company for the Colt Single Action Army revolver. Adopted by the U.S. Army in 1873, the cartridge and its iconic revolver became synonymous with the American frontier. The .45 Colt saw extensive service during the Indian Wars, and its heavy 255-grain lead bullet at roughly 850 fps earned a fearsome reputation for stopping power.

The cartridge served as the U.S. military's primary sidearm round until the adoption of the .38 Long Colt in 1892 — a decision that was eventually reversed when the .38 proved inadequate during the Philippine-American War, leading to the development of the .45 ACP. Despite being technically superseded, the .45 Colt never disappeared. Its association with the Old West kept it alive through the 20th century, and the nickname ".45 Long Colt" persists in common usage despite being technically incorrect, originating from the need to distinguish it from the shorter .45 Schofield cartridge.

## Modern Usage

The .45 Colt has experienced a remarkable renaissance driven by cowboy action shooting sports and the continued popularity of single-action revolvers. Organizations like the Single Action Shooting Society (SASS) have created a thriving competitive community built around Old West firearms and cartridges, with the .45 Colt as a primary chambering. Manufacturers like Ruger, Uberti, and Pietta produce a wide variety of revolvers in the caliber.

Beyond nostalgia, the .45 Colt remains a practical cartridge. In strong modern revolvers like the Ruger Blackhawk, handloaders can push the .45 Colt to near-.44 Magnum performance levels, making it a capable hunting cartridge. The Taurus Judge and Smith & Wesson Governor, which chamber both .45 Colt and .410 bore shotshells, brought new attention to the cartridge. Lever-action rifles in .45 Colt from Henry and others serve as effective brush guns for deer-sized game.
