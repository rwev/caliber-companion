## History

The .450 Marlin was developed in 2000 through a collaboration between Marlin Firearms and Hornady Manufacturing to solve a persistent safety problem with the .45-70 Government. For decades, ammunition manufacturers and handloaders had been pushing the .45-70 far beyond its original black-powder pressure levels in strong modern rifles like the Marlin 1895 and Ruger No. 1, while the same cartridge also had to remain safe in antique trapdoor Springfield rifles dating to 1873. This created a confusing and potentially dangerous situation where vastly different pressure levels of ammunition existed for the same cartridge, with no reliable way to prevent hot loads from being chambered in rifles that could not handle them.

The .450 Marlin solved this by creating a distinct cartridge that could only chamber in rifles designed for it. Built on a belted case derived from the .458 Winchester Magnum shortened to 2.10 inches, the belt prevents the cartridge from chambering in .45-70 rifles. This allowed SAAMI to standardize the cartridge at 43,500 psi — roughly matching the hottest .45-70 handloads — without any risk of the ammunition finding its way into an unsafe firearm. Marlin introduced the cartridge in their 1895M lever-action rifle with an 18.5-inch ported barrel, positioning it as the ultimate guide gun for dangerous game encounters in thick cover.

## Modern Usage

The .450 Marlin occupies a specialized role as a close-range powerhouse for situations where maximum stopping power matters more than flat trajectory or long-range precision. With Hornady's 325-grain FTX LEVERevolution load generating over 3,500 ft-lbs of muzzle energy, the cartridge hits harder at the muzzle than a .300 Winchester Magnum and maintains authority on large game out to 200 yards — about the maximum practical range for the lever-action rifles it is typically chambered in. The LEVERevolution line was particularly significant for the cartridge, as the pointed flex-tip bullets dramatically improved the ballistic coefficient and safe-in-tube-magazine design extended effective range compared to traditional flat-point loads.

The cartridge found its primary audience among hunters in Alaska and the northern Rocky Mountain states who need a compact, fast-handling rifle capable of stopping brown bears, moose, and bison at close quarters. The short, lightweight Marlin 1895M — despite its punishing recoil of approximately 35 ft-lbs in a seven-pound rifle — became a popular guide gun and backcountry companion. However, the .450 Marlin has struggled to achieve mainstream adoption. Ammunition selection remains limited, with Hornady being essentially the sole manufacturer. Marlin's ownership transitions — from Remington to Ruger — disrupted rifle production for years. Many shooters who understand the pressure caveats simply opt for .45-70 Government, which offers similar performance in modern rifles along with far broader ammunition and firearm availability. The .450 Marlin remains a sound design that solved a real problem, but one that the market has largely chosen to address through education rather than a new cartridge.
