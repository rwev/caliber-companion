## History

The 9x18mm Makarov was developed in 1946 by Boris Semin as part of a Soviet program to replace the aging 7.62x25mm Tokarev cartridge with a round better suited to compact, simple blowback-operated pistols. The cartridge was adopted in 1951 alongside the Makarov PM pistol designed by Nikolai Makarov. The design intentionally used a bullet diameter of 9.27mm (.365 inches), slightly larger than the 9mm Luger's 9.01mm (.355 inches), ensuring that captured NATO ammunition could not be fired in Soviet weapons — and vice versa.

The 9mm Makarov became the standard sidearm cartridge for the Soviet military, the KGB, and Soviet police forces. As the Cold War unfolded, every Warsaw Pact nation adopted the cartridge, and it was manufactured in enormous quantities in the Soviet Union, East Germany, Czechoslovakia, Bulgaria, China, and other aligned nations. The Makarov PM pistol, praised for its simplicity and reliability, was produced in the millions and became one of the most recognizable handguns of the 20th century.

## Modern Usage

Following the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991, enormous quantities of surplus Makarov pistols and ammunition flooded Western markets, creating a new generation of enthusiasts. The Makarov PM and its variants — including the excellent CZ 82 from Czechoslovakia and the East German Pistole M — developed a cult following among American shooters who appreciated their build quality, accuracy, and historical significance, all at surplus prices.

The 9mm Makarov occupies a ballistic niche between the .380 ACP and 9mm Luger, offering modestly better performance than the .380 in a similarly compact platform. While no major Western manufacturer currently produces new firearms in the caliber, the enormous installed base of surplus pistols sustains ongoing demand for ammunition. Several manufacturers, including Hornady, Fiocchi, and various Eastern European producers, continue to offer 9x18mm Makarov ammunition. The cartridge remains in active military and police service in several former Soviet-bloc nations that have not yet completed transitions to NATO-standard 9x19mm.
