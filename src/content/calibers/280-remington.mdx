## History

The .280 Remington was introduced in 1957 as Remington's answer to the .270 Winchester, which had dominated the high-velocity sporting cartridge market for over three decades. Based on the .30-06 Springfield case necked down to 7mm (.284 caliber), the .280 Remington offered access to the superb selection of 7mm bullets — many of which featured higher ballistic coefficients and sectional densities than their .277-caliber counterparts — while maintaining similar velocity and energy. On paper, it was arguably the superior cartridge.

Unfortunately, the .280 Remington suffered from a series of marketing missteps that hampered its commercial success. Remington initially introduced it in their semi-automatic Model 740 rifle rather than a bolt-action, and loaded it to slightly lower pressures to ensure reliable function in the autoloader — a decision that cost it velocity compared to the .270 Winchester. In 1979, Remington attempted to revive interest by renaming the cartridge the "7mm Express Remington," which only created confusion with the vastly more popular 7mm Remington Magnum and angered existing .280 owners. The name was changed back to .280 Remington in 1981, but the damage to market perception had been done.

## Modern Usage

Despite never achieving the sales figures of the .270 Winchester or .30-06 Springfield, the .280 Remington has earned a devoted following among knowledgeable hunters and ballistics enthusiasts who recognize it as one of the finest all-around hunting cartridges ever designed. Modern factory ammunition is loaded to higher pressures than the original offerings, extracting the cartridge's full potential and closing the velocity gap with the .270 Winchester. With 140-grain bullets at 3,000 fps, it is a superb deer and pronghorn cartridge; with 160-grain premium bullets, it is a thoroughly capable elk and moose round. The .280 Remington benefits enormously from the excellent selection of high-BC 7mm bullets available today, many of which were designed for precision rifle competition and bring outstanding long-range performance. Several premium rifle makers, including Kimber and Nosler, chamber their flagship hunting rifles in .280 Remington, reflecting the cartridge's sterling reputation among serious hunters.
