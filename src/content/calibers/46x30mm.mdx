## History

The 4.6×30mm cartridge was developed in the late 1990s by Heckler & Koch in response to a NATO requirement for a new personal defense weapon system capable of defeating modern body armor. The concept emerged from a post-Cold War reassessment of the threat environment facing rear-echelon military personnel — vehicle crews, signallers, logistics troops, and officers — who needed a compact, lightweight weapon more effective against armored threats than the standard 9mm pistol. NATO formalized this requirement in 2003 under document D/296, and two competing systems emerged: the FN P90 chambered in 5.7×28mm and the HK MP7 chambered in the new 4.6×30mm.

Heckler & Koch designed the 4.6×30mm around a small, bottlenecked case that propels a lightweight projectile at very high velocity. The standard military loading — the DM11 — fires a 31-grain copper-plated steel-core armor-piercing bullet at approximately 2,380 feet per second from the MP7's 7.1-inch barrel. This combination was designed to penetrate the NATO CRISAT target (a 1.6mm titanium plate backed by 20 layers of Kevlar, simulating typical body armor of the era) at 200 meters. The resulting NATO evaluation pitted the 4.6×30mm against FN's 5.7×28mm in extensive testing that lasted years. While the final assessment found the 5.7×28mm slightly superior in terminal performance, Germany and several other nations ultimately selected the HK MP7 system, driven in part by the weapon platform's exceptional compactness and ergonomics.

## Modern Usage

The 4.6×30mm is fielded almost exclusively in the HK MP7, which remains the only production firearm chambered for the cartridge. This limitation is both the round's defining characteristic and its primary constraint. The MP7 is an extraordinarily compact weapon — smaller than most submachine guns when its stock is collapsed and its foregrip folded — yet it delivers rifle-like armor penetration from a package little larger than a pistol. This makes it ideal for VIP protection details, vehicle crews, special operations personnel who need a concealable yet capable weapon, and tactical law enforcement units operating in confined spaces. Germany's elite GSG 9 counter-terrorism unit, various NATO special operations forces, and reportedly the U.S. Secret Service have adopted the MP7 system.

The civilian market for 4.6×30mm is extremely limited. HK does not offer a semi-automatic civilian version of the MP7, and no other manufacturer produces firearms in the caliber. Ammunition availability is restricted largely to Fiocchi, which produces a 40-grain FMJ training load, and surplus military ammunition that occasionally enters the commercial market. The cartridge has no practical civilian application beyond novelty, as the platform that makes it effective — the select-fire MP7 — is unavailable to most civilian buyers. Within its intended military and law enforcement role, however, the 4.6×30mm represents a thoughtful engineering solution to the specific problem of personal defense against armored threats, trading the raw stopping power of larger calibers for armor penetration, controllability, and ammunition capacity in the smallest possible package.
