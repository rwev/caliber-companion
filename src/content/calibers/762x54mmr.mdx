## History

The 7.62×54mmR was adopted by the Russian Empire in 1891 alongside the Mosin-Nagant bolt-action rifle, making it one of the earliest smokeless-powder military cartridges in the world. Designed by a Russian military commission, the round featured a distinctive rimmed case that simplified extraction in bolt-action rifles and belt-fed machine guns. It served as Russia's primary rifle cartridge through the Russo-Japanese War, World War I, the Russian Revolution, and the brutal fighting on the Eastern Front of World War II, where millions of Mosin-Nagant rifles and thousands of machine guns consumed staggering quantities of ammunition.

The cartridge's longevity is remarkable. While most of its contemporaries — the 8mm Lebel, 6.5mm Arisaka, and .30-40 Krag — have long since been retired from military service, the 7.62×54mmR endured because the Soviets wisely retained it for machine guns and precision rifles even as they adopted intermediate cartridges for infantry rifles. The PKM general-purpose machine gun and the SVD Dragunov sniper rifle, both adopted in the 1960s, ensured the cartridge's continued relevance in a modern military context.

## Modern Usage

Today the 7.62×54mmR remains the standard machine gun and designated marksman cartridge of the Russian military, fed through the PKM, PKP Pecheneg, and SVD platforms. It has seen extensive combat use in the conflicts in Ukraine, Syria, and throughout the post-Soviet sphere. On the civilian surplus market, the cartridge gained enormous popularity due to the flood of inexpensive Mosin-Nagant rifles and crates of surplus ammunition that entered Western markets after the fall of the Soviet Union. Hunters have found the round effective on deer, elk, and other medium to large game, delivering performance broadly comparable to the .30-06 Springfield. Commercial manufacturers like PPU and Sellier & Bellot produce sporting ammunition, ensuring the cartridge's viability beyond the dwindling surplus supply.
