## History

The .450 Bushmaster traces its lineage to Colonel Jeff Cooper's "Thumper" concept — the idea of a large-caliber cartridge capable of taking any North American game out to 250 yards from a standard AR-15-size platform. Tim LeGendre of LeMag Firearms developed the original .45 Professional cartridge to meet this concept, and Bushmaster Firearms worked with Hornady to refine the design into what became the .450 Bushmaster in 2007. The cartridge uses a rebated-rim case based on the .284 Winchester, necked straight to accept .452-caliber pistol bullets. The case length was designed to fit within the AR-15's magazine well and overall length constraints, while the large case capacity allowed velocities exceeding 2,200 fps with 250-grain projectiles.

For several years the .450 Bushmaster existed as a niche big-bore AR-15 option, popular mainly among hog hunters and those who simply wanted maximum stopping power from the platform. Its fortunes changed dramatically when Michigan approved straight-wall cartridge rifles for deer hunting in 2014, followed by other Midwestern states. Suddenly the .450 Bushmaster had a massive new market of hunters who could now use a rifle instead of a shotgun slug, and the cartridge's availability in both AR-15 and bolt-action platforms made it extremely appealing.

## Modern Usage

The .450 Bushmaster has become the dominant big-bore straight-wall cartridge in the Midwest, particularly in Michigan where it enjoys near-iconic status among deer hunters. Ruger's American Ranch rifle in .450 Bushmaster became one of the best-selling deer rifles in the region, and multiple AR-15 manufacturers offer dedicated upper receivers for the cartridge. The combination of .45-caliber bullets at rifle velocities produces devastating terminal performance on deer-sized game, with typical shots resulting in very short tracking distances. The cartridge is also effective on larger game including black bear and elk within its effective range. The primary drawback is recoil — significantly more than a .308 Winchester and uncomfortable for some shooters in lightweight rifles — and the rainbow trajectory limits practical range to about 200-250 yards. Ammunition is available from Hornady, Federal, Winchester, and Remington, though selection is more limited and prices are higher than mainstream rifle cartridges.
