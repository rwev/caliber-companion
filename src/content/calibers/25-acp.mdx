## History

The .25 ACP (6.35mm Browning) was designed by John Moses Browning in 1905 and introduced alongside the FN Model 1905 Vest Pocket pistol. Browning's intent was to create the smallest practical semi-automatic pistol cartridge, enabling a new class of ultra-compact firearms that could be carried invisibly in a vest pocket or handbag. The cartridge was a commercial success in Europe, where it became the standard chambering for an entire generation of tiny pocket pistols.

Throughout the first half of the 20th century, .25 ACP pistols were enormously popular as gentlemen's pocket guns, ladies' purse guns, and backup weapons for military officers and law enforcement. The cartridge armed countless small pistols from manufacturers including FN, Beretta, Colt, and Walther. During both World Wars, compact .25 ACP pistols served as personal defense weapons for officers, pilots, and intelligence operatives who needed a concealable firearm that would not interfere with their primary duties.

## Modern Usage

The .25 ACP occupies a diminished but historically significant place in the modern firearms landscape. The rise of compact .380 ACP and even subcompact 9mm pistols that are barely larger than the old .25 ACP pocket guns has largely rendered the cartridge obsolete for practical self-defense purposes. Its energy levels — typically under 65 ft-lbs at the muzzle — place it at the very bottom of what could be considered a viable defensive cartridge.

Despite this, the .25 ACP retains a following among collectors and enthusiasts who appreciate the elegant little pistols it was designed for. The Beretta 21A Bobcat remains in production, and vintage .25 ACP pistols are popular collector's items. Ammunition remains available from major manufacturers, though at prices that often exceed those of far more capable cartridges — a reflection of the low production volumes rather than any inherent manufacturing cost. For most practical purposes, the .25 ACP has been superseded by the .380 ACP in the pocket pistol role.
