## History

The .45-70 Government was developed at the U.S. Army's Springfield Armory in 1873 for use in the Springfield Model 1873 "Trapdoor" rifle, becoming the standard U.S. military cartridge for nearly two decades. Its original designation described the key specifications: a .45-caliber bullet propelled by 70 grains of black powder. The cartridge saw extensive service throughout the Indian Wars, was carried by troops at the Battle of the Little Bighorn in 1876, and served through the Spanish-American War. It was eventually replaced by the .30-40 Krag in 1892 as the military transitioned to smokeless powder and smaller-bore cartridges, but the .45-70 never disappeared from civilian use.

Throughout the 20th century, the cartridge maintained a loyal following among hunters who appreciated its ability to push heavy, large-diameter bullets through dense brush. The advent of modern smokeless powder loads dramatically increased its performance, and Marlin's Model 1895 lever-action rifle became the quintessential .45-70 platform. The Hornady LEVERevolution line, introduced in 2005 with pointed polymer-tipped bullets safe for tubular magazines, gave the old cartridge a meaningful ballistic improvement and renewed commercial interest.

## Modern Usage

Today the .45-70 Government enjoys a strong renaissance as a hunting cartridge for North American big game, particularly in thick timber and brush where shots are typically under 200 yards. Its heavy bullets — commonly 300 to 405 grains — deliver devastating terminal performance on elk, moose, and black bear, and "+P" loads from manufacturers like Buffalo Bore and Garrett Cartridges push the cartridge into territory suitable for dangerous game including grizzly bear and bison. The .45-70 is a popular choice for guides in Alaska and bear country as a close-range stopping rifle.

Beyond hunting, the .45-70 occupies a unique niche in cowboy action shooting and among enthusiasts of classic American firearms. Reproduction Sharps and Trapdoor Springfield rifles chambered in .45-70 are popular for long-range black powder cartridge competition. The cartridge's moderate chamber pressures in standard loads make it safe for antique and reproduction firearms, while modern lever-action and single-shot rifles can handle significantly hotter loads. This versatility across pressure levels and platforms is a defining characteristic that has kept the .45-70 relevant for over 150 years.
