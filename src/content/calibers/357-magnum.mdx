## History

The .357 S&W Magnum was born from a collaboration between Elmer Keith, Phil Sharpe, and Smith & Wesson's Douglas Wesson in the early 1930s. Wildcatters had been pushing the .38 Special beyond its intended pressures for years, and the goal was to create a factory cartridge that safely delivered those high-performance loads. By lengthening the .38 Special case by approximately one-tenth of an inch, the new cartridge could not be chambered in older .38 Special revolvers, preventing dangerous overpressure situations.

Smith & Wesson introduced the .357 Magnum in 1935 with the Registered Magnum revolver, later renamed the Model 27. It was an immediate sensation, adopted by the FBI and numerous law enforcement agencies. For decades, the .357 Magnum was the gold standard for police duty revolvers. The cartridge also found a home in lever-action rifles, where longer barrels extracted substantially higher velocities and made it an effective short-range deer cartridge.

## Modern Usage

The .357 Magnum remains one of the most popular revolver cartridges in the world. While semi-automatic pistols have largely replaced revolvers in law enforcement, the .357 Magnum thrives in the civilian market for self-defense, hunting, and recreational shooting. Its versatility is unmatched — any .357 Magnum revolver can also fire milder .38 Special ammunition, giving shooters a wide spectrum of power levels from a single firearm.

Modern defensive loads from manufacturers like Hornady, Speer, and Federal deliver devastating terminal performance with controlled expansion. For hunters, heavier 180-grain hard-cast loads are effective on deer and black bear at moderate ranges. The cartridge also enjoys popularity in lever-action rifles from Henry and Marlin, where it serves as a capable brush gun. The continued production of new revolvers from Smith & Wesson, Ruger, and Colt ensures the .357 Magnum's place as a cornerstone of American shooting culture.
