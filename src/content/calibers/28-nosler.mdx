## History

The .28 Nosler was introduced by Nosler, Inc. in 2015 as the second cartridge in the company's proprietary family of high-performance hunting rounds, following the .26 Nosler (2014). The cartridge was designed around a non-belted case derived from the .404 Jeffery, featuring a rebated rim and generous case capacity that allows it to push 7mm bullets approximately 200 to 300 fps faster than the venerable 7mm Remington Magnum. Nosler designed it to fit standard-length (.30-06 length) bolt actions, making it compatible with the widest range of rifle platforms.

The .28 Nosler arrived at a time when long-range hunting was experiencing explosive growth in popularity, and its timing proved excellent. Hunters and shooters who wanted to extract maximum performance from the outstanding selection of high-BC 7mm bullets found the .28 Nosler to be an ideal platform. The cartridge quickly earned SAAMI standardization and gained chambering options from several prominent rifle manufacturers, including Christensen Arms, Browning, and of course Nosler's own Model 48 rifles. While it has not achieved the mainstream ubiquity of the 7mm Rem Mag, it has developed a passionate following among performance-oriented hunters.

## Modern Usage

The .28 Nosler is positioned as a premium long-range hunting cartridge, and its users tend to be serious hunters who are willing to accept increased recoil, shorter barrel life, and higher ammunition cost in exchange for superior downrange performance. The cartridge excels at pushing high-BC 7mm bullets — such as the 162-grain Hornady ELD-X, 168-grain Berger VLD, and 175-grain Nosler AccuBond Long Range — at velocities that maximize their aerodynamic advantages. At 500 yards and beyond, the .28 Nosler retains significantly more energy and produces less wind drift than the 7mm Rem Mag.

The primary trade-offs of the .28 Nosler are barrel life and recoil. The cartridge's large case capacity and high velocities result in barrel life of approximately 800 to 1,200 rounds, roughly half that of the 7mm Rem Mag. Recoil is also notably stiffer at approximately 27 ft-lbs of free recoil energy. Ammunition availability has improved since the cartridge's introduction, with Nosler, Hornady, and other manufacturers offering both hunting and match loads. The .28 Nosler faces increasing competition from the 7mm PRC, which was introduced in 2022 with strong industry backing from Hornady. However, the .28 Nosler's velocity advantage ensures it remains the choice for hunters who want the absolute maximum performance from a 7mm-bore cartridge in a standard-length action.
