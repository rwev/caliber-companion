## History

The 28 gauge follows the traditional British gauge system, with its bore diameter of 0.550 inches (14.0mm) corresponding to a lead ball weighing one twenty-eighth of a pound. Like most shotgun gauges, the 28 bore was established during the 19th century, though it remained a relative obscurity for much of its early existence. The gauge found its first dedicated following among American upland hunters in the early 20th century, but production was limited and ammunition options were few. For decades, the 28 gauge was widely dismissed as too small for serious hunting, suitable only for experienced shooters looking for a challenge or for introducing children to the shotgun sports.

The 28 gauge's reputation began to change in the 1960s and 1970s as competitive skeet shooters discovered its remarkable patterning characteristics. Because the 28 gauge's three-quarter-ounce payload passes through a relatively small bore, the shot column is shorter and less deformed than in larger gauges, resulting in patterns that are often more uniform and efficient than their payload size would suggest. Skeet shooters began reporting that their 28 gauge scores were approaching and sometimes matching their 12 gauge performance at regulation distances, leading to the widely repeated claim that the 28 gauge "shoots above its weight." This reputation attracted a growing cadre of enthusiasts who appreciated the gauge not despite its limitations but because of the rewarding experience it provided.

## Modern Usage

The 28 gauge has experienced significant growth in both manufacturer support and shooter interest over the past two decades. Nearly every major shotgun manufacturer now offers at least one model in 28 gauge, with over-under and semi-automatic configurations being the most popular. The Browning Citori, Beretta 686 Silver Pigeon, and Benelli Ultra Light are among the most sought-after 28 gauge platforms. Modern 28 gauge guns typically weigh between five and six pounds, making them the lightest practical repeating shotguns available and a joy to carry on extended upland hunts.

In the field, the 28 gauge excels on small to medium upland birds — quail, dove, woodcock, and grouse are its ideal quarry. The three-quarter-ounce payload of number six or seven-and-a-half shot delivers effective patterns to 30 yards, which covers the vast majority of flushing bird shots. Some adventurous hunters push the 28 gauge into pheasant territory with success, though this demands disciplined shot selection and acceptance of a shorter effective range. In clay target sports, the 28 gauge has dedicated events in skeet competition and is increasingly popular in informal sporting clays outings. The shooting experience is genuinely pleasant — recoil is minimal, muzzle blast is reduced, and the light guns swing quickly and instinctively. Ammunition selection has expanded considerably, with Federal, Winchester, Fiocchi, and others offering both field and target loads. The 28 gauge will never be a mainstream choice, but its devoted following continues to grow among shooters who have discovered that less can indeed be more.
