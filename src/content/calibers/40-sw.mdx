## History

The .40 S&W was born out of the aftermath of the 1986 FBI Miami shootout, in which two FBI agents were killed and five wounded in a gunfight that exposed the limitations of the Bureau's 9mm and .38 Special service ammunition. The FBI initially adopted the 10mm Auto, but its full-power loads proved too punishing for many agents. Working with Smith & Wesson and Winchester, the FBI developed a reduced-power 10mm "Lite" load. Smith & Wesson and Winchester then shortened the 10mm case to fit in a 9mm-sized frame, and the .40 S&W was introduced on January 17, 1990.

The cartridge was an immediate commercial success. By the mid-1990s, the majority of U.S. law enforcement agencies had transitioned from 9mm or .357 Magnum revolvers to .40 S&W semi-automatics. The round offered greater energy than the 9mm while maintaining a manageable recoil envelope and fitting into compact duty pistols.

## Modern Usage

The .40 S&W's dominance in law enforcement began to wane after the FBI's 2014 decision to return to 9mm, citing advances in modern hollow-point bullet technology that effectively closed the terminal performance gap. Many agencies followed suit, and the trend accelerated through the late 2010s and early 2020s. Despite this shift, the .40 S&W remains a very common cartridge with a large installed base of firearms and a loyal following among civilian shooters.

The round continues to see use among agencies that have not yet transitioned, and it remains a popular choice for personal defense among those who prefer a larger-caliber semi-automatic. Surplus .40 S&W duty pistols from police trade-ins represent excellent values on the used market, sustaining ongoing demand for ammunition and keeping the cartridge firmly in the mainstream.
