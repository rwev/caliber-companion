## History

The 5.45×39mm was developed in the early 1970s as the Soviet Union's response to the American adoption of the 5.56×45mm cartridge during the Vietnam War. Soviet military analysts observed that the small-caliber, high-velocity concept allowed soldiers to carry more ammunition with less fatigue while maintaining effective terminal performance at typical combat distances. The resulting cartridge was paired with the AK-74 rifle — a redesigned AK-47 chambered for the new round — and both were adopted in 1974. The standard military projectile, designated 7N6, featured a distinctive design with an air pocket in the nose and a mild steel core, which caused the bullet to yaw rapidly upon impact, earning it the nickname "the poison bullet" from Afghan mujahideen who encountered it during the Soviet-Afghan War beginning in 1979.

The 5.45×39mm quickly became the standard Soviet infantry cartridge, replacing the 7.62×39mm in frontline rifle units. Warsaw Pact allies adopted it in various quantities, and production expanded across the Eastern Bloc. The cartridge proved effective in the harsh conditions of Afghanistan, where the AK-74's improved accuracy and flatter trajectory over the older AKM were appreciated by Soviet soldiers.

## Modern Usage

The 5.45×39mm remains the standard Russian military rifle cartridge, serving in the AK-74M and its modernized derivatives. It has seen extensive combat use in Chechnya, Syria, Ukraine, and other conflicts involving Russian and post-Soviet forces. On the civilian market, the cartridge gained popularity in the West through imported AK-74 variants and surplus 7N6 ammunition, though the ATF's 2014 ban on 7N6 imports as "armor-piercing pistol ammunition" significantly impacted the surplus supply in the United States. Commercial ammunition from manufacturers like Hornady and Wolf keeps the round available for civilian shooters, and AK-74-pattern rifles remain popular among enthusiasts who appreciate the platform's extremely low recoil and fast follow-up shot capability.
