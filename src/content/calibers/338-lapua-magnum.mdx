## History

The .338 Lapua Magnum originated from a joint development effort in the 1980s between the Finnish ammunition manufacturer Lapua and the American firearms research firm Research Armament Industries (RAI). The project was driven by a U.S. military requirement for a long-range sniping cartridge that could bridge the gap between the 7.62 NATO and the .50 BMG, providing accurate anti-personnel capability at ranges beyond 1,000 meters in a rifle that a single operator could carry. Early development used the .416 Rigby case necked down to .338 caliber, and after resolving initial case-head separation issues, Lapua redesigned the case with a stronger head and introduced the finalized cartridge in 1989.

The .338 Lapua Magnum gained international military prominence when the British Armed Forces adopted it in the Accuracy International L115A3 rifle. In 2009, British Corporal of Horse Craig Harrison set a confirmed kill record at 2,475 meters using this platform in Afghanistan, dramatically demonstrating the cartridge's extreme-range capability. Since then, numerous NATO and allied nations have adopted the .338 Lapua in various sniper systems, and it has become the de facto standard for long-range military sniping below the .50-caliber threshold.

## Modern Usage

In modern military service, the .338 Lapua Magnum is fielded by special operations forces and designated marksmen across dozens of nations. Platforms like the Barrett MRAD, Accuracy International AXMC, and Sako TRG 42 provide sub-MOA precision at ranges that would be impractical with 7.62 NATO or even .300 Win Mag. The cartridge's combination of high ballistic coefficient bullets, substantial case capacity, and moderate barrel wear compared to overbore designs makes it a practical choice for sustained operational use.

In the civilian world, the .338 Lapua has become the premier cartridge for extreme long-range (ELR) competition, where targets are regularly engaged beyond 1,500 yards. It is also used by a smaller number of hunters pursuing large or dangerous game at long range, though its significant recoil and ammunition cost limit its appeal for general hunting. The cartridge requires magnum-length actions and heavier rifles to manage recoil, and ammunition prices typically start around $3.50 per round for match-grade loads. Despite these considerations, the .338 Lapua Magnum has cemented its place as one of the most capable and respected long-range cartridges ever developed.
