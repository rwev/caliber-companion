## History

The 16 gauge has roots stretching back to the earliest days of standardized shotgun bore sizes in the 19th century. Under the gauge system, a 16 gauge bore accepts a lead ball weighing one-sixteenth of a pound, yielding a bore diameter of 0.662 inches (16.83mm). In the golden age of American shotgunning — roughly the 1920s through the 1950s — the 16 gauge enjoyed enormous popularity and was widely considered the ideal all-around upland hunting gauge. The Browning Auto-5 "Sweet Sixteen," introduced in 1936, became one of the most beloved shotguns in American sporting history and cemented the 16 gauge's reputation as the thinking hunter's choice. Manufacturers like Parker, L.C. Smith, Fox, and Ithaca all offered their finest side-by-side and pump-action designs in 16 gauge.

The 16 gauge's decline began in the 1960s and accelerated through the following decades, driven largely by the ammunition industry's decision to load 16 gauge shells in 12 gauge-sized hulls rather than developing true 16 gauge-proportioned components. This meant that 16 gauge guns had to be built on heavier 12 gauge frames, negating the weight advantage that had been the bore size's primary selling point. Simultaneously, improvements in 20 gauge ammunition narrowed the performance gap from below, while 12 gauge magnum loads expanded the larger bore's versatility. Caught in a squeeze between its neighbors, the 16 gauge saw its market share shrink dramatically, and many manufacturers dropped it from their lineups entirely.

## Modern Usage

The 16 gauge is experiencing a modest but genuine revival driven by nostalgia, renewed manufacturer interest, and a community of dedicated hunters who never abandoned the bore. Browning reintroduced the A5 Sweet Sixteen in 2016, this time built on a proper 16 gauge-proportioned receiver rather than a scaled-down 12 gauge frame, addressing the historical complaint that had undermined the gauge for decades. Other manufacturers including Franchi, CZ, and several Italian makers have followed with 16 gauge offerings in both semi-automatic and over-under configurations. These modern guns weigh around six to six and a half pounds, hitting a sweet spot between the typical 12 gauge at seven-plus pounds and the 20 gauge at five and a half.

The 16 gauge's practical niche is upland bird hunting, where its one-ounce payload delivers meaningfully denser patterns than the 20 gauge's standard seven-eighths ounce load while producing noticeably less recoil than the 12 gauge. For pheasant, grouse, quail, and woodcock, the 16 gauge is an excellent choice that rewards good shooting without punishing the shooter. Ammunition availability, while not matching the 12 or 20 gauge, has improved considerably with the resurgence of interest. Federal, Fiocchi, RST, and several European manufacturers offer quality 16 gauge hunting loads. The gauge will likely never reclaim its mid-century prominence, but it has found a sustainable place as a connoisseur's choice for hunters who appreciate tradition, balanced handling, and the satisfaction of carrying something slightly different afield.
