## History

The .380 ACP (Automatic Colt Pistol) was designed by John Moses Browning and introduced in 1908 by Colt for the Colt Model 1908 pocket hammerless pistol. Known in Europe as the 9mm Short or 9mm Browning Short, the cartridge was designed to provide adequate self-defense performance in a blowback-operated semi-automatic small enough to slip into a coat pocket.

The .380 ACP saw widespread military adoption across Europe in the first half of the 20th century. It was the standard chambering for numerous iconic pistols, including the Walther PP and PPK, the Beretta M1934, and the Browning Model 1910 — the pistol used to assassinate Archduke Franz Ferdinand in 1914, the event that precipitated World War I. During World War II, the .380 served as a secondary military cartridge for several nations on both sides of the conflict.

## Modern Usage

The .380 ACP experienced a dramatic resurgence in popularity beginning around 2008 with the introduction of the Ruger LCP, which kicked off a wave of ultra-compact, lightweight pocket pistols. Today, the cartridge is one of the most popular choices for concealed carry, particularly among shooters who prioritize minimal size and weight or who are recoil-sensitive.

Modern defensive ammunition from manufacturers like Hornady, Federal, and Speer has significantly improved the .380's terminal performance, though it still falls short of the 9mm in raw energy. The cartridge fills an important niche as the minimum caliber widely recommended for personal defense, offering a meaningful step up from .22 LR and .25 ACP while remaining manageable in the smallest semi-automatic pistols available. Its low recoil makes it particularly popular among new shooters and those with reduced hand strength.
