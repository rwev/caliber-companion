## History

The .50 Browning Machine Gun cartridge was developed in the closing stages of World War I by John Moses Browning at the request of General John J. Pershing, who sought a heavy machine gun capable of defeating aircraft and lightly armored vehicles. Browning scaled up his proven .30-06 cartridge design, and the resulting .50 caliber round was formally adopted in 1921 alongside the M2 heavy machine gun. The M2 "Ma Deuce" went on to become one of the longest-serving weapon systems in U.S. military history, seeing continuous combat deployment from World War II through every American conflict to the present day. The cartridge's combination of extreme range, penetration, and destructive energy made it indispensable for anti-aircraft defense, vehicle armament, and infantry support roles.

The .50 BMG's second chapter began in the early 1980s when Ronnie Barrett, a civilian photographer with no formal engineering training, designed the first shoulder-fired semi-automatic rifle chambered in .50 BMG — the Barrett Model 82. Military snipers quickly recognized the platform's potential for anti-materiel missions, and the Barrett M82A1 (later designated M107) was adopted by the U.S. military and dozens of allied forces. Carlos Hathcock's legendary 2,286-yard kill in Vietnam — achieved with an M2 machine gun fitted with a scope — had already demonstrated the cartridge's extreme-range precision potential decades earlier.

## Modern Usage

The .50 BMG remains the dominant heavy machine gun cartridge in NATO service, with the M2 Browning still mounted on vehicles, aircraft, and naval vessels worldwide. In the anti-materiel and extreme long-range sniping role, the Barrett M82A1/M107 and McMillan TAC-50 are standard-issue platforms in Western militaries. The TAC-50 holds the record for the longest confirmed sniper kill in history — a 3,540-meter shot by a Canadian special forces operator in Iraq in 2017. Military applications include disabling vehicles, detonating unexploded ordnance at safe distances, engaging targets behind cover, and destroying communications equipment.

In the civilian market, .50 BMG rifles occupy a unique niche among extreme long-range shooting enthusiasts. Single-shot bolt actions like the Serbu RN-50 and Armalite AR-50 offer more affordable entry points into the caliber, while the Barrett M82A1 remains the aspirational standard. Ammunition costs are substantial, typically ranging from three to eight dollars per round, and the rifles themselves are heavy, loud, and demand significant shooting space. Despite these barriers, the .50 BMG community is dedicated, with organized competitions extending past 1,000 yards. The cartridge is legal for civilian ownership in most U.S. states, though California notably banned .50 BMG rifles in 2004.
