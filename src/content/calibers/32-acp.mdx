## History

The .32 ACP (7.65mm Browning) was designed by John Moses Browning in 1899 and introduced with the FN Model 1900, the first commercially successful semi-automatic pistol. The cartridge was designed for simple blowback operation, eliminating the need for a locked breech and allowing pistols to be built with fewer parts, lighter weight, and greater reliability than the revolvers of the era.

The .32 ACP became the de facto standard European military and police sidearm cartridge for decades. It armed some of the most iconic pistols in firearms history, including the Walther PP and PPK, the Browning Model 1910, the Beretta M1934, and the CZ vz. 27. During World War II, millions of .32 ACP pistols were carried by officers, NCOs, and intelligence operatives on all sides of the conflict. The Walther PPK in .32 ACP became the most famous fictional firearm in history as the signature weapon of James Bond, chosen by the character on the recommendation of firearms expert Geoffrey Boothroyd.

## Modern Usage

The .32 ACP has largely been eclipsed by the .380 ACP and 9mm in practical terms, but it maintains a dedicated following. The cartridge offers an interesting middle ground — more powerful than the .25 ACP, with less recoil than the .380 ACP — that appeals to shooters seeking maximum controllability in a small package. The Kel-Tec P-32, one of the lightest semi-automatic pistols ever made at just 6.6 ounces, demonstrates that the .32 ACP still has a place in the ultra-lightweight concealed carry niche.

European manufacturers like Sellier & Bellot and GECO continue to produce .32 ACP ammunition in quantity, keeping it affordable and available. The cartridge also benefits from an enormous global supply of surplus and vintage firearms, ensuring that demand for ammunition will persist. For collectors and enthusiasts of classic European firearms, the .32 ACP remains an essential cartridge, connecting modern shooters to the golden age of pocket pistol design.
