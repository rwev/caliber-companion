## History

The .375 Ruger was jointly developed by Hornady and Ruger in 2007 as a modern alternative to the venerable .375 H&H Magnum. The design brief was ambitious: match or exceed the ballistic performance of the .375 H&H while fitting into a standard-length bolt action rather than requiring the longer magnum-length receiver. Engineers achieved this by using a fatter, beltless case with a wider body diameter of 0.532 inches at the rim, which provided greater powder capacity in a shorter package. The result was a cartridge with a 2.580-inch case that could propel a 300-grain bullet at 2,660 fps — essentially duplicating the .375 H&H in a more compact platform.

Ruger chambered the cartridge in their Hawkeye African and Guide Gun models, while Hornady provided the initial ammunition lineup. The cartridge met the minimum energy requirements for dangerous game in most African countries on arrival, positioning it as a legitimate safari round from day one. While it has not displaced the deeply entrenched .375 H&H, it has carved out a loyal following among hunters who appreciate the advantages of a standard-length action.

## Modern Usage

Today the .375 Ruger serves primarily as a dangerous game and large-bodied game cartridge. In Africa, it meets the legal minimum of .375 caliber required for hunting Cape buffalo, elephant, and other dangerous species in most countries, making it a practical choice for safari hunters who want a lighter rifle. In North America, it has found a strong niche among guides and hunters in Alaska and British Columbia for protection against and hunting of large brown bears and grizzlies, where its combination of power and relatively compact rifle dimensions is advantageous in thick brush.

The cartridge benefits from an excellent selection of heavy, controlled-expansion and solid bullets from manufacturers including Hornady, Barnes, and Swift. Hornady's Dangerous Game Series ammunition with DGX bonded expanding and DGS solid bullets provides reliable safari-grade terminal performance. While ammunition availability is more limited than the .375 H&H, the major manufacturers maintain consistent production. The .375 Ruger represents a practical, modern approach to dangerous game cartridge design that continues to gain acceptance among serious big-game hunters.
