## History

The .22 Long Rifle was developed in 1887 by the J. Stevens Arms & Tool Company by combining the case of the .22 Long with the 40-grain bullet of the .22 Extra Long. This simple innovation created what would become the most widely produced and consumed ammunition cartridge in the history of firearms. As a rimfire cartridge — where the priming compound is contained in the rim of the case rather than a separate primer — the .22 LR is inherently inexpensive to manufacture.

By the early 20th century, the .22 LR had become the standard training cartridge for militaries worldwide. It served as the foundation for generations of Olympic and competitive shooting disciplines, and its minimal report and recoil made it the default choice for introducing new shooters to the sport. The cartridge has been chambered in virtually every type of firearm action ever devised, from single-shot target pistols to lever-action rifles to semi-automatic handguns and long guns.

## Modern Usage

The .22 LR remains the highest-volume ammunition cartridge sold worldwide, with billions of rounds produced annually. Its appeal is rooted in three unbeatable advantages: extremely low cost, negligible recoil, and extraordinary versatility. A box of 500 rounds of .22 LR often costs less than a single box of 50 rounds of centerfire ammunition, making it the most economical way to develop and maintain shooting skills.

In the handgun world, the .22 LR serves primarily as a training and recreational cartridge. Dedicated .22 pistols like the Ruger Mark IV and Browning Buck Mark are staples of range bags everywhere, and conversion kits allow shooters to practice with their carry guns at a fraction of the cost of centerfire ammunition. The cartridge also supports a thriving competitive ecosystem, from NRA Bullseye to Steel Challenge. While not recommended as a primary self-defense caliber, the .22 LR fills an important role for those who cannot manage the recoil of larger cartridges.
