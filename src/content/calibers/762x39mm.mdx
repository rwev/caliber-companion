## History

The 7.62×39mm cartridge was developed during World War II by Soviet designers who studied the German 7.92×33mm Kurz intermediate cartridge concept. Officially adopted in 1944, the round was designated the M43 and represented a deliberate compromise between the full-power 7.62×54mmR rifle cartridge and the 7.62×25mm Tokarev pistol round. It was initially chambered in the SKS carbine, but its true claim to fame came with the adoption of the AK-47 assault rifle by Mikhail Kalashnikov in 1949. The pairing of this reliable intermediate cartridge with one of the most rugged firearms ever designed created a weapons system that would define armed conflict for the next half-century.

Throughout the Cold War, the Soviet Union exported the AK-47 and its ammunition to allied nations, revolutionary movements, and insurgent groups worldwide. China produced it as the Type 56, and dozens of countries from Egypt to North Korea manufactured their own variants. By the 1970s, the 7.62×39mm was the most widely distributed military cartridge on the planet, found in virtually every armed conflict from Vietnam to Angola to Afghanistan.

## Modern Usage

Although the Russian military largely transitioned to the 5.45×39mm with the adoption of the AK-74 in 1974, the 7.62×39mm remains in widespread global service. It continues to be the primary rifle cartridge for numerous militaries across Africa, Asia, and the Middle East, and vast stockpiles of surplus ammunition ensure its availability for decades to come. In the civilian market, the cartridge has found a strong following in the United States and elsewhere, chambered in AK-pattern rifles, the SKS, and modern platforms like the Ruger Mini-30 and various AR-15-pattern rifles. Its moderate recoil, adequate terminal performance on medium game, and historically low ammunition cost make it a practical choice for hunting, target shooting, and home defense.
