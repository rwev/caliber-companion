## History

The .357 SIG was developed jointly by SIG Sauer and Federal Cartridge in 1994 with a singular goal: replicate the terminal ballistics of the legendary 125-grain .357 Magnum load — widely considered the most effective handgun manstopper ever documented — in a semi-automatic pistol cartridge. The designers achieved this by necking down a .40 S&W case to accept a 9mm (.355-inch) bullet, creating a bottleneck cartridge that drives lighter bullets to unusually high velocities for a handgun round.

The cartridge's bottleneck design provides an additional benefit beyond raw velocity: exceptional feeding reliability. The tapered case guides smoothly into the chamber, virtually eliminating the feed malfunctions that can occur with straight-walled cartridges. This reliability advantage, combined with impressive ballistic performance, attracted the attention of several prominent federal law enforcement agencies.

## Modern Usage

The .357 SIG has carved out a distinct niche in the law enforcement world. The U.S. Secret Service adopted it in the late 1990s, and the Federal Air Marshal Service selected it for its superior barrier penetration and incapacitation potential in the confined environment of an aircraft cabin. Several state police agencies, including the Texas Department of Public Safety and Virginia State Police, also adopted the cartridge.

Despite its strong performance credentials, the .357 SIG has never achieved widespread civilian popularity. Higher ammunition costs, sharper recoil compared to 9mm, reduced magazine capacity versus .40 S&W, and limited firearm options have kept it as a niche choice. However, those who do choose the .357 SIG tend to be devoted enthusiasts who appreciate its flat trajectory, barrier-blind performance, and the confidence that comes from carrying a cartridge trusted by some of the nation's most demanding protective agencies.
