## History

The 9×19mm Parabellum was designed by Georg Luger in 1901 and introduced in 1902 for the German Navy's Luger P08 pistol. Derived from the earlier 7.65×21mm Parabellum, Luger necked up the case to accept a 9mm bullet, creating a cartridge that balanced terminal performance with controllable recoil in a compact package. The name "Parabellum" comes from the Latin *si vis pacem, para bellum* — "if you seek peace, prepare for war."

Germany adopted it as their standard military sidearm cartridge during World War I, and its use spread rapidly across Europe during the interwar period. By World War II, the 9mm was standard issue for most Axis and many Allied forces. NATO formally standardized the cartridge in 1955, cementing its dominance in Western military circles.

## Modern Usage

Today the 9mm Luger is the undisputed king of handgun cartridges. The FBI's 2014 decision to return to 9mm — after years with the .40 S&W — catalyzed a near-universal shift among American law enforcement agencies. Modern bullet technology, particularly bonded and engineered hollow points like the Federal HST and Speer Gold Dot, closed the terminal performance gap that once justified larger calibers.

For civilian shooters, the 9mm offers an unmatched combination of low recoil, high capacity, wide ammunition availability, and affordable training costs. It dominates competitive shooting disciplines like USPSA Production and IDPA, and remains the most recommended caliber for concealed carry. The global ammunition market produces more 9mm than any other centerfire cartridge, ensuring consistent availability and competitive pricing.
