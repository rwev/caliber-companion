## History

The .41 Remington Magnum was introduced in 1964, born from the advocacy of gun writers Elmer Keith, Bill Jordan, and Skeeter Skelton. These influential figures argued that law enforcement needed a cartridge more powerful than the .357 Magnum but more controllable than the .44 Magnum. Their vision was a .41-caliber round loaded to two levels: a full-power hunting load and a reduced-velocity police load with a lead semi-wadcutter bullet.

Smith & Wesson chambered the cartridge in their Model 57 (target model with adjustable sights) and Model 58 (fixed-sight service model). However, the plan faltered in execution. The revolvers were built on the same large N-frame as the .44 Magnum, making them too large and heavy for comfortable police duty carry. The intended mild police load was seldom produced, and the full-power loads generated recoil that most officers found excessive. Law enforcement agencies largely passed on the .41 Magnum, and without that market anchor, the cartridge settled into niche status.

## Modern Usage

Despite its commercial underperformance, the .41 Magnum has maintained a dedicated following among handgun hunters and revolver aficionados who recognize it as a ballistically excellent cartridge. It offers a genuine performance advantage over the .357 Magnum — roughly 40% more energy — while producing noticeably less recoil than the .44 Magnum. For deer-sized game, many experienced hunters consider it the ideal revolver cartridge.

Ammunition availability is more limited than the .357 or .44 Magnum, and factory offerings come primarily from specialty manufacturers like Buffalo Bore and DoubleTap, with periodic offerings from Federal and Hornady. Handloaders have access to excellent .41-caliber bullet options and find the cartridge exceptionally accurate. The Ruger Blackhawk and Redhawk remain available in .41 Magnum, and used Smith & Wesson Model 57 and 58 revolvers command premium prices from collectors who appreciate this underrated cartridge.
