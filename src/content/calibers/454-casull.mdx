## History

The .454 Casull was developed in 1957 by Dick Casull and Jack Fullmer in Utah. Working from the .45 Colt case, Casull lengthened it slightly, strengthened the case head, and used small rifle primers to withstand the dramatically higher pressures — over 65,000 psi, nearly double that of the .44 Magnum. The result was a cartridge that propelled a 260-grain bullet at over 1,800 fps from a revolver, a performance level previously unheard of in a handgun.

For decades, the .454 Casull existed only as a wildcat cartridge, available in custom five-shot revolvers built by Freedom Arms in Freedom, Wyoming. The Freedom Arms Model 83 revolver, introduced in 1983, was the first commercially available firearm chambered for the round and quickly earned a reputation as one of the finest revolvers ever manufactured. The Sporting Arms and Ammunition Manufacturers' Institute (SAAMI) officially standardized the cartridge in 1997, opening the door for other manufacturers to chamber firearms in the caliber.

## Modern Usage

The .454 Casull serves a specific but devoted user base. Its primary role is as a handgun hunting cartridge capable of taking any game animal in North America, and as a bear defense round in areas inhabited by grizzlies and brown bears. The cartridge generates energy levels comparable to many rifle cartridges, with 300-grain loads producing over 1,500 ft-lbs at the muzzle.

Revolvers chambered in .454 Casull can also fire .45 Colt and, in some cases, .45 Schofield ammunition, providing versatility for practice and lighter-duty applications. The Ruger Super Redhawk, Taurus Raging Bull, and Magnum Research BFR offer more affordable alternatives to the premium Freedom Arms revolvers. While the punishing recoil limits the .454 Casull's appeal to experienced shooters, those who master it gain one of the most capable handgun cartridges available.
