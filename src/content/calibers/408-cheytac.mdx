## History

The .408 CheyTac was developed in 2001 by Dr. John D. Taylor and machinist William O. Wordman as a purpose-built extreme long-range cartridge designed to fill the gap between the .338 Lapua Magnum and the .50 BMG. The project began with a thorough analysis of transonic flight instability — the tendency of bullets to become erratic as they decelerate through the speed of sound — and worked backward to engineer a cartridge and bullet combination that would remain supersonic and stable past 2,000 yards. The result was the .408 CheyTac, based on a necked-down and lengthened .505 Gibbs case, paired with precision lathe-turned solid copper projectiles featuring an exceptionally high ballistic coefficient.

CheyTac Associates introduced the cartridge alongside the M200 Intervention rifle system, which included an integrated ballistic computer designed to compensate for temperature, altitude, humidity, Coriolis effect, and spin drift at extreme distances. The system gained widespread public attention through its portrayal in films and video games, but its real-world credentials were established through military evaluations and record-breaking long-range shots. In 2002, the CheyTac system demonstrated consistent hits on a human-silhouette target at 2,321 yards during U.S. Navy testing.

## Modern Usage

The .408 CheyTac occupies a highly specialized niche in the long-range shooting world. Its primary advantage over the .50 BMG is efficiency: the .408 CT delivers comparable or superior ballistic performance at extreme range with roughly half the recoil, significantly less rifle weight, and better inherent accuracy. The cartridge remains supersonic past 2,200 yards under standard atmospheric conditions, and its purpose-designed projectiles maintain stability through the transonic zone far better than conventional .50 caliber bullets. This makes the .408 CheyTac arguably the most capable dedicated anti-personnel sniping cartridge in existence at distances beyond one mile.

Military adoption has been selective but notable. Special operations units in several countries have evaluated or fielded .408 CheyTac systems, and the Turkish military manufactures rifles chambered for the cartridge domestically. The civilian market for .408 CheyTac is small but passionate, consisting primarily of extreme long-range competition shooters and enthusiasts who demand the ultimate in extended-range performance. Ammunition availability is limited compared to mainstream calibers, with most loads produced by specialty manufacturers. Costs are high, typically six to twelve dollars per round, and rifle options are few. Despite these barriers, the .408 CheyTac has earned a devoted following among shooters who measure their engagements in miles rather than yards.
