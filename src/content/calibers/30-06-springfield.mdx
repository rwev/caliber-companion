## History

The .30-06 Springfield was developed at the Springfield Armory in 1906 as an improvement over the .30-03 cartridge that had briefly served in the M1903 Springfield rifle. The redesign featured a lighter 150-grain spitzer (pointed) bullet at higher velocity, bringing American military ballistics in line with contemporary European developments. The cartridge saw its baptism of fire in World War I, where it proved devastating in both the M1903 Springfield bolt-action and the M1917 Enfield. Its reputation was cemented in World War II as the chambering for the legendary M1 Garand — the first semi-automatic rifle issued as a standard infantry weapon — as well as the M1919 Browning medium machine gun and the BAR automatic rifle.

After the war, the U.S. military transitioned to the shorter 7.62×51mm NATO in the 1950s, but the .30-06 had already secured an unshakeable place in American civilian shooting culture. Generations of returning veterans were intimately familiar with the cartridge, and the massive surplus of military rifles and ammunition made it accessible to hunters across the country. Its ability to handle bullet weights from 110 to 220 grains gave it a versatility few cartridges could match, suitable for everything from varmints to moose.

## Modern Usage

Today the .30-06 Springfield remains one of the best-selling rifle cartridges in America, despite being well over a century old. Every major rifle manufacturer chambers bolt-action hunting rifles in .30-06, and ammunition is available in an enormous variety of loadings from every major manufacturer. With 150-grain bullets it is a superb deer cartridge with a flat trajectory; loaded with 180- or 200-grain controlled-expansion bullets, it is fully capable on elk, moose, and large African plains game. The cartridge's broad versatility means a hunter with a single .30-06 rifle can pursue virtually any game animal in North America. While newer short-magnum cartridges have eroded some of its market share, the .30-06 endures because of its proven track record, wide availability, manageable recoil in standard-weight rifles, and the simple fact that it does everything well.
