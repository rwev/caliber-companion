## History

The .327 Federal Magnum was introduced in 2008 by Federal Premium Ammunition in partnership with Sturm, Ruger & Co. It represented the culmination of a lineage of progressively more powerful .32-caliber revolver cartridges: the .32 S&W, .32 S&W Long, .32 H&R Magnum, and finally the .327 Federal Magnum. By lengthening and strengthening the .32 H&R Magnum case and substantially increasing chamber pressure to 45,000 psi, Federal created a cartridge that approached .357 Magnum energy levels with significantly less recoil.

The key innovation was practical rather than purely ballistic. The smaller .32-caliber bore allowed Ruger to fit six chambers into their SP101 revolver — a frame that holds only five rounds of .357 Magnum. This extra round represented a meaningful capacity advantage for a defensive revolver. The cartridge also offered impressive velocity, with 100-grain bullets exceeding 1,500 fps from short barrels, producing flat trajectories and authoritative terminal performance.

## Modern Usage

The .327 Federal Magnum occupies a thoughtful niche in the revolver world. Its primary appeal is as a defensive cartridge for shooters who want more power than a .38 Special but find the .357 Magnum's recoil excessive, particularly in lightweight snub-nose revolvers. The combination of six-round capacity, moderate recoil, and genuine magnum-level ballistics makes a compelling case for personal protection.

The cartridge's versatility is enhanced by its backward compatibility — any .327 Federal Magnum revolver can fire .32 H&R Magnum, .32 S&W Long, and .32 S&W ammunition, providing an exceptionally wide range of power levels from a single firearm. Henry produces a lever-action rifle in .327 Federal Magnum that serves as a capable small game and varmint gun. However, limited ammunition availability compared to the .357 Magnum remains the cartridge's primary challenge, and shooters are well advised to handload or stock up when factory ammunition is available.
