## History

The 8mm Mauser cartridge has its origins in 1888, when the German military adopted the 7.92×57mm round with a 0.318-inch diameter bullet (designated the "I" or "infantry" bore) for the Gewehr 88 Commission Rifle. In 1905, Germany modernized the cartridge by increasing the bullet diameter to 0.323 inches and adopting a pointed (spitzer) bullet, creating the "S Patrone" — the 7.92×57mm JS that would serve as the definitive version. This updated cartridge became one of the most important military rounds of the 20th century, seeing action in both World Wars chambered in the Gewehr 98, Karabiner 98k, MG 08, MG 34, and the fearsome MG 42.

During World War II, the 8mm Mauser was produced in staggering quantities. Germany's wartime allies and satellite states — including Turkey, Yugoslavia, Romania, and others — also adopted Mauser-pattern rifles and the cartridge, ensuring its widespread distribution. After the war, enormous stockpiles of captured German weapons and ammunition were distributed globally, and production continued in countries like Yugoslavia, Turkey, and Egypt for decades.

## Modern Usage

While no major military currently fields the 8mm Mauser as a primary cartridge, it remains a popular round among surplus firearms collectors, sport shooters, and hunters. The Karabiner 98k is one of the most collected military rifles in the world, and Yugoslav M48 Mausers offer an affordable entry point for enthusiasts. As a hunting cartridge, the 8mm Mauser is highly capable — its heavy 196-grain bullets deliver excellent penetration and terminal performance on deer, elk, and moose at moderate ranges. European hunters, particularly in Scandinavia and Central Europe, continue to use it in both military surplus and commercial sporting rifles. Ammunition is readily available from manufacturers like PPU, Sellier & Bellot, and Norma, though American domestic loads are often loaded conservatively due to concerns about older rifles with the smaller-bore "I" designation.
