## History

The .303 British was adopted in 1889 as part of the transition from black powder to smokeless-powder cartridges, initially loaded with compressed black powder and a 215-grain round-nose bullet for the Lee-Metford rifle. The cartridge evolved rapidly — cordite propellant replaced black powder in 1891, and the adoption of a 174-grain spitzer bullet (the Mk VII) in 1910 brought the round to its mature, highly effective form. The .303 became synonymous with the Short Magazine Lee-Enfield (SMLE), whose smooth bolt action allowed trained soldiers to deliver an astonishing rate of accurate fire. At the Battle of Mons in 1914, German troops famously mistook rapid Lee-Enfield fire for machine guns.

Through both World Wars, the .303 served across every theater in rifles, light machine guns (Bren), heavy machine guns (Vickers), and aircraft-mounted weapons. It was the standard cartridge not only of Britain but of the entire Commonwealth — Canada, Australia, India, New Zealand, South Africa, and dozens of other nations relied on it. The cartridge served the British military until 1957, when NATO standardization led to its replacement by the 7.62×51mm NATO.

## Modern Usage

The .303 British retains a devoted following among military surplus collectors and hunters, particularly in Commonwealth nations where it has a deep cultural legacy. In Canada, Australia, and parts of Africa, it remains a popular deer and medium-game hunting cartridge, prized for the reliability of the Lee-Enfield action and the effectiveness of modern expanding ammunition. Commercial loads from Hornady, PPU, Sellier & Bellot, and others keep the cartridge viable, and handloaders appreciate the case's generous capacity. While surplus military ammunition has largely dried up, the sheer number of Lee-Enfield rifles produced — over 17 million — ensures ongoing demand for .303 British ammunition worldwide.
