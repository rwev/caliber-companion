## History

The 10mm Auto has one of the most colorful origin stories in firearms history. The concept was championed by Colonel Jeff Cooper, the legendary firearms instructor and founder of Gunsite Academy, who envisioned a semi-automatic pistol cartridge that could match the .357 Magnum in terminal performance. Swedish ammunition manufacturer Norma developed the cartridge in 1983, and it was first chambered in the Bren Ten pistol produced by Dornaus & Dixon — a gun that gained fame as the sidearm of Sonny Crockett on the television show *Miami Vice*, though it was plagued with production issues.

The cartridge's most significant adoption came in 1989, when the FBI selected the Smith & Wesson Model 1076 in 10mm Auto following the devastating 1986 Miami shootout. However, the full-power 10mm proved too powerful for many agents, leading to reduced-power "10mm Lite" loads that eventually spawned the .40 S&W. The FBI abandoned the 10mm by 1997, and the cartridge faded into relative obscurity for over a decade.

## Modern Usage

The 10mm Auto has experienced a remarkable renaissance since the mid-2010s, driven largely by the growing interest in handgun hunting and backcountry bear defense. Major manufacturers including Glock, SIG Sauer, Springfield Armory, and Smith & Wesson have introduced new 10mm pistols, and ammunition options have expanded dramatically.

The cartridge occupies a unique niche as the most powerful mainstream semi-automatic handgun round. With full-power loads pushing 200-grain hardcast bullets past 1,200 fps, the 10mm generates energy levels approaching the .357 Magnum while offering the higher capacity and faster reloads of a semi-automatic platform. It has become a favorite among hikers, hunters, and outdoorsmen in bear country, and enjoys a dedicated following among competitive shooters and handgun enthusiasts who appreciate its ballistic versatility.
