## History

The .45 ACP (Automatic Colt Pistol) was developed by John Moses Browning in 1905 at the request of the U.S. Military, which sought a more effective sidearm cartridge after the .38 Long Colt proved inadequate against Moro guerrillas during the Philippine-American War. The Thompson-LaGard tests of 1904 concluded that a .45 caliber bullet was the minimum acceptable for a military handgun, and Browning designed the cartridge alongside his iconic M1911 pistol. The combination was formally adopted by the U.S. Army in 1911 and went on to serve through both World Wars, Korea, and Vietnam.

During World War II the .45 ACP saw widespread use not only in the 1911 pistol but also in the M1A1 Thompson submachine gun and the M3 "Grease Gun." Its heavy 230-grain bullet at subsonic velocities earned a reputation for reliable stopping power at close range. The cartridge remained the standard U.S. military sidearm round until it was officially replaced by the 9mm NATO with the adoption of the Beretta M9 in 1985, though special operations units continued to use .45 ACP platforms for years afterward.

## Modern Usage

Today the .45 ACP remains one of the most popular handgun cartridges in the United States. While the broader law enforcement and military trend has shifted toward 9mm for its higher capacity and lower recoil, the .45 retains a dedicated following among shooters who value its large frontal diameter and heavy bullet momentum. Modern defensive loads from Federal, Hornady, and Speer have further refined its terminal performance, making it a capable self-defense choice in full-size and compact pistols alike. The 1911 platform continues to thrive in both duty and competition circles, and manufacturers like Glock, SIG Sauer, and HK offer modern polymer-framed pistols chambered in .45 ACP for those who prefer the cartridge in a contemporary design.
