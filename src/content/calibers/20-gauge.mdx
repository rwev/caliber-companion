## History

The 20 gauge shotgun follows the same centuries-old British gauge system as its larger siblings, with the bore diameter determined by the number of lead balls of that diameter equaling one pound — in this case, twenty balls, yielding a nominal bore diameter of 0.615 inches (15.63mm). Like the 12 gauge, the 20 gauge became standardized during the 19th century as British and European gunmakers refined the breech-loading shotgun. The smaller bore was always intended as a lighter, more portable alternative to the 12 gauge, and it found its earliest champions among upland bird hunters who valued a lighter gun for long days walking through fields and forests.

Throughout the 20th century, the 20 gauge steadily grew in popularity as ammunition technology improved. Advances in powder formulations, wad design, and shot materials allowed 20 gauge loads to achieve performance levels that would have been unthinkable in earlier decades. The development of 3-inch magnum 20 gauge shells in particular narrowed the payload gap with standard 2¾-inch 12 gauge loads, giving the smaller bore genuine versatility across a wider range of hunting applications. By the mid-20th century, the 20 gauge had firmly established itself as the standard recommendation for youth shooters, smaller-framed adults, and anyone seeking reduced recoil without abandoning the shotgun platform entirely.

## Modern Usage

The 20 gauge is the clear second choice in the shotgun world, trailing only the 12 gauge in popularity but holding a strong and growing market share. Its appeal centers on the fundamental advantage of reduced recoil — roughly 30 to 40 percent less than a comparable 12 gauge load — combined with noticeably lighter firearms. Modern 20 gauge semi-automatics from manufacturers like Benelli, Beretta, and Browning weigh as little as five and a half pounds, making them ideal for all-day upland hunts where every ounce matters. In the clay target sports, the 20 gauge has its own dedicated classes in skeet competition, and many recreational trap and sporting clays shooters prefer it for the gentler shooting experience.

For home defense, the 20 gauge has gained significant ground as a practical alternative to the 12 gauge, particularly for household members who find 12 gauge recoil excessive. Loaded with #3 buckshot, the 20 gauge delivers meaningful terminal performance at room distances while remaining controllable for shooters of varying size and strength. The Mossberg 500 and Remington 870 are both available in dedicated 20 gauge configurations with shorter barrels and tactical stocks. As a hunting cartridge, the 20 gauge handles virtually everything the 12 gauge can, with the caveat that maximum effective range and payload capacity are somewhat reduced. For upland birds, waterfowl over decoys, turkey within reasonable range, and deer with slugs or buckshot, the 20 gauge is entirely adequate and considerably more pleasant to shoot.
