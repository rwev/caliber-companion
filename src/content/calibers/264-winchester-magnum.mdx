## History

Winchester introduced the .264 Winchester Magnum in 1959 as part of a new family of belted magnum cartridges based on a shortened .375 H&H case. It was designed to be the flattest-shooting commercial cartridge available, pushing 6.5mm (.264-caliber) bullets to velocities that would not be matched by mainstream cartridges for decades. The cartridge was initially chambered in the Winchester Model 70 Westerner with a 26-inch barrel, where it could launch 140-grain bullets at over 3,000 fps — extraordinary performance for the era.

The .264 Win Mag enjoyed a brief period of popularity in the early 1960s among western hunters pursuing mule deer, antelope, and sheep at long range across open terrain. However, several factors conspired against its long-term success. Winchester inexplicably began offering it in rifles with 22-inch barrels, which cost 100-150 fps and negated much of the cartridge's velocity advantage. The heavy powder charges burned barrels quickly, with accurate barrel life often limited to 800-1,000 rounds. Perhaps most damaging, the bullet technology of the era could not fully exploit the cartridge's potential — the high-BC, heavy-for-caliber 6.5mm bullets that make modern 6.5mm cartridges so effective simply did not exist in the 1960s. By the time the 7mm Remington Magnum arrived in 1962, the .264 was already fading from the spotlight.

## Modern Usage

The .264 Winchester Magnum occupies a curious position in the modern cartridge landscape: a niche classic that was vindicated by the very trends that passed it by. The explosion of interest in 6.5mm cartridges driven by the 6.5 Creedmoor, 6.5 PRC, and .26 Nosler has proven that shooters value exactly the attributes the .264 Win Mag offered decades ago — flat trajectory, high ballistic coefficients, moderate recoil for a magnum, and excellent wind-bucking ability. With modern slow-burning powders and high-BC bullets from Berger, Hornady, and Nosler, the .264 Win Mag can achieve performance that rivals or exceeds the 6.5 PRC.

Factory ammunition remains available but limited, with Nosler and Winchester being the primary sources. The cartridge has a devoted following among handloaders who appreciate its large case capacity and the performance it unlocks with modern components. However, for shooters building a new rifle, the 6.5 PRC offers very similar ballistics in a more efficient, shorter-action package with better barrel life and broader industry support. The .264 Winchester Magnum endures as a testament to forward-thinking cartridge design and remains a capable choice for hunters who already own one or who appreciate its place in firearms history.
