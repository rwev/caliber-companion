## History

The 10 gauge traces its origins to the early standardization of shotgun bore sizes in the 19th century, with a bore diameter of 0.775 inches (19.69mm) corresponding to a lead ball weighing one-tenth of a pound. For much of the black powder era, the 10 gauge was a standard waterfowling bore, prized by market hunters and sportsmen alike for its ability to throw heavy charges of shot at passing ducks and geese. Early 10 gauge shotguns — often massive double-barreled side-by-sides — were the long-range weapons of choice for hunters shooting from sink boxes and layout blinds on the Chesapeake Bay and other major flyways. The bore was common enough to be considered a mainstream option rather than a specialty item.

The 10 gauge's trajectory shifted dramatically in 1991 when federal regulations banned the use of lead shot for waterfowl hunting in the United States, mandating steel shot as a nontoxic alternative. Steel shot is significantly less dense than lead, resulting in reduced energy and shorter effective range for a given pellet size. This disadvantage hit smaller gauges hardest and gave the 10 gauge a renewed practical advantage: its larger bore and heavier payloads could compensate for steel's deficiencies by throwing more pellets at higher velocities. The introduction of the Remington SP-10 semi-automatic and Browning BPS pump action in 10 gauge during this era gave waterfowlers modern, reliable platforms for the big bore, and the 10 gauge found a new niche as the specialist's waterfowl gun.

## Modern Usage

The 10 gauge occupies a narrow but dedicated niche in the modern shotgun market, serving primarily as a waterfowl and turkey hunting tool. Its core advantage is payload capacity: a standard 10 gauge 3½-inch shell can deliver two ounces of steel shot, significantly more than even a 12 gauge 3½-inch magnum. For pass-shooting geese at extended range, where pattern density at 50 to 60 yards determines whether birds are cleanly taken or merely scared, the 10 gauge offers a meaningful edge. Turkey hunters also appreciate the 10 gauge's ability to throw dense patterns of large shot at longer range, extending ethical kill distances beyond what the 12 gauge can reliably achieve.

The practical market for 10 gauge firearms has contracted substantially, however. The introduction of 12 gauge 3½-inch magnum chambers — pioneered by the Mossberg 835 and Benelli Super Black Eagle — gave 12 gauge shotguns much of the payload capacity that was once the 10 gauge's exclusive domain. Modern tungsten-based nontoxic shot materials like Tungsten Super Shot and HEVI-Shot have further eroded the 10 gauge's advantage by delivering lead-like density from smaller gauge platforms. Today, firearm options in 10 gauge are limited primarily to the Browning BPS and Gold semi-automatic, with the Remington SP-10 having been discontinued. Ammunition selection is modest compared to the 12 gauge but adequate, with Federal, Remington, and several specialty manufacturers offering waterfowl and turkey loads. The 10 gauge endures for hunters who demand the absolute maximum in downrange payload delivery and are willing to accept the weight, recoil, and limited firearm selection that come with it.
