## History

The .410 bore is unique among common shotgun chamberings in that it is designated by caliber rather than gauge. If measured under the traditional gauge system, the .410 bore would be approximately 36 gauge — the number of .410-diameter lead balls required to make one pound. The cartridge originated in the United Kingdom around 1874, initially offered as a short brass-cased round for small garden guns and collector firearms. Early .410 shells were only two inches in length and carried minimal shot payloads, limiting the cartridge to pest control and very close-range small game. Over the following decades, longer shell lengths were introduced — the 2½-inch shell became standard, followed by the 3-inch magnum — gradually expanding the .410's practical capability.

Throughout the 20th century, the .410 bore carved out a clear identity as the beginner's and expert's shotgun shell. Youth shooters valued its negligible recoil in lightweight single-shot and pump-action guns, while experienced shotgunners embraced it as the ultimate test of skill in clay target sports. Shooting .410 at skeet became a prestigious discipline, as the tiny payload and sparse pattern demanded precision that larger gauges forgave. The .410 also maintained a quiet but steady following among small game hunters in rural areas, where its light report and minimal shot charge were ideal for dispatching rabbits, squirrels, and pests at close range.

## Modern Usage

The .410 bore experienced a dramatic surge in commercial interest in 2006 with the introduction of the Taurus Judge revolver, a five-shot handgun chambered to fire both .410 shotshells and .45 Colt cartridges. The Judge became one of the best-selling handguns in America and spawned imitators including the Smith and Wesson Governor. These revolvers transformed the .410 from a niche shotgun round into a popular self-defense cartridge, though the terminal effectiveness of .410 handgun loads from short revolver barrels remains a subject of considerable debate among ballistic experts. Specialty ammunition manufacturers responded with optimized .410 self-defense loads featuring stacked projectile designs and plated disc-and-ball combinations.

In its traditional long-gun role, the .410 continues to serve as an ideal introductory shotgun for young and small-framed shooters. The Mossberg 500 .410, Henry Single Shot, and various youth-model pump actions provide a low-recoil platform for learning shotgun fundamentals before graduating to larger gauges. In competitive shooting, the .410 remains the smallest gauge used in formal skeet competition, and accomplished shooters take pride in mastering its demanding patterns. For small game and pest control, the .410 is adequate within its limited effective range of roughly 25 yards with shot loads. The ammunition market has expanded significantly thanks to the revolver-driven demand, and .410 shells are now readily available in most sporting goods stores at reasonable prices.
