## History

The .350 Legend was introduced by Winchester Ammunition at the 2019 SHOT Show, designed to address a specific regulatory opportunity in the American hunting landscape. Several Midwestern states — including Michigan, Ohio, Indiana, and Iowa — had historically restricted deer hunting to shotguns only, but began amending their regulations to allow straight-wall cartridge rifles. This opened the door for cartridges like the .450 Bushmaster and .45-70 Government, but Winchester saw an opportunity to create something purpose-built: a straight-wall cartridge with lower recoil, flatter trajectory, and less cost per round than the existing big-bore options. The result was the .350 Legend, built on a case with the same rim diameter as the .223 Remington, allowing it to function in AR-15-pattern rifles and existing short-action bolt guns with minimal modification.

Winchester's marketing emphasized that the .350 Legend offered more energy than the .30-30 Winchester, less recoil than the .243 Winchester, and a lower price point than nearly any centerfire hunting cartridge. The straight-wall case design ensured compliance with state regulations while the moderate pressures kept recoil manageable for youth, new, and recoil-sensitive hunters.

## Modern Usage

The .350 Legend has found its primary audience among deer hunters in straight-wall-restricted states, where it offers clear advantages over both shotgun slugs and the larger straight-wall cartridges. Its moderate recoil makes it an excellent choice for youth hunters and anyone who finds the .450 Bushmaster or .45-70 uncomfortable, while its effective range of roughly 200-250 yards covers the vast majority of whitetail hunting scenarios in the Midwest. The cartridge is available in both bolt-action and AR-15 platforms, with Winchester, Ruger, Savage, and CMMG all offering rifles. Ammunition is widely available and competitively priced, with options from Winchester, Federal, Hornady, and others covering everything from inexpensive range loads to premium hunting projectiles. Outside of the straight-wall states, the cartridge faces stiff competition from established bottleneck cartridges like the .30-30 and .243 Winchester, but it continues to grow as more states revise their hunting regulations.
