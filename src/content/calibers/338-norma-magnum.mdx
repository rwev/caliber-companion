## History

The .338 Norma Magnum was developed by the Swedish ammunition manufacturer Norma Projektilfabrik in collaboration with American competitive shooter Jimmie Sloan and Finnish rifle maker Sako. Introduced in 2009, the cartridge was designed to address a specific limitation of the popular .338 Lapua Magnum: its long case often prevented the seating of heavy, high-ballistic-coefficient .338 caliber bullets at optimal depth while still fitting within standard-length magnum actions. The .338 Norma Magnum solved this by using a shorter, fatter case based on the .416 Rigby parent case, necked down to .338 caliber. This geometry allowed long, heavy projectiles like the 300-grain Sierra MatchKing to be seated at their ideal depth while maintaining compatibility with standard long-action receivers.

The cartridge's trajectory changed dramatically in 2017 when U.S. Special Operations Command selected the .338 Norma Magnum as the chambering for its Advanced Sniper Rifle program, ultimately fielded as the Mk 22 ASR built on the Barrett MRAD platform. This decision validated the cartridge's design philosophy and elevated it from a niche competitive round to a military-adopted precision cartridge. SOCOM's selection was driven by the .338 NM's ability to deliver consistent extreme long-range performance with slightly less overall length than the .338 Lapua Magnum, allowing better compatibility with multi-caliber chassis systems.

## Modern Usage

The .338 Norma Magnum's most significant modern role is as the primary chambering for the U.S. SOCOM Mk 22 Advanced Sniper Rifle system. The Barrett MRAD-based platform allows operators to switch between .338 Norma Magnum for extreme long-range engagements and .300 Norma Magnum or 7.62 NATO through barrel and bolt changes, but the .338 NM serves as the primary long-range configuration. The cartridge offers effective engagement capability past 1,500 yards, with heavy 300-grain match projectiles maintaining supersonic velocity well beyond that distance. Several NATO allies have followed SOCOM's lead in evaluating or adopting .338 NM platforms.

In the civilian precision rifle community, the .338 Norma Magnum is gaining traction among extreme long-range competitors and enthusiasts who want military-grade ballistic performance. The cartridge's compatibility with standard long-action magazines and its efficient case design make it somewhat more practical than the .338 Lapua Magnum in certain rifle platforms. Ammunition availability has improved significantly since the SOCOM adoption, with Norma, Hornady, and several specialty manufacturers offering match-grade loads. The recoil is substantial — roughly 35 foot-pounds in a typical 14- to 15-pound rifle — but manageable with modern muzzle brakes and chassis systems. For shooters seeking a proven military-grade long-range cartridge that bridges the gap between .300 Winchester Magnum and .50 BMG, the .338 Norma Magnum has become a compelling option.
