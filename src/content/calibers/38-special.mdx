## History

The .38 Smith & Wesson Special was introduced in 1898 as an improvement over the .38 Long Colt, which had proven inadequate during the Philippine-American War. Smith & Wesson lengthened the case and increased the powder charge, creating a cartridge that offered meaningfully better ballistic performance while remaining compatible with medium-frame revolvers. The U.S. Army adopted it in 1899, and it quickly became the dominant American handgun cartridge.

For the better part of a century, the .38 Special was synonymous with the American police revolver. From the 1920s through the early 1990s, the vast majority of U.S. law enforcement officers carried a .38 Special revolver, most commonly a Smith & Wesson Model 10 or Colt Official Police. The introduction of +P loads in the 1970s extended the cartridge's service life by providing higher velocities and improved terminal performance from standard-pressure revolvers rated for the increased pressure.

## Modern Usage

Although semi-automatic pistols chambered in 9mm have largely replaced .38 Special revolvers in law enforcement and military roles, the cartridge retains a strong civilian following. Snub-nose revolvers like the Smith & Wesson J-frame series and the Ruger LCR remain extremely popular concealed carry choices, prized for their simplicity, reliability, and ease of use under stress.

The .38 Special is also a favorite among target shooters and recreational plinkers due to its mild recoil and inherent accuracy. It serves as an ideal training cartridge for shooters who also own .357 Magnum revolvers, since any revolver chambered in .357 Magnum can also fire .38 Special ammunition at reduced recoil and cost. The enormous variety of available loads — from light wadcutters to hot +P defensive rounds — makes it one of the most versatile handgun cartridges ever produced.
