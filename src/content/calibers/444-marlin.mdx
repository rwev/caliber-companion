## History

The .444 Marlin was introduced in 1964 as a joint development between Marlin Firearms and Remington. The cartridge was created to give lever-action shooters a modern, high-performance big-bore option at a time when the .45-70 Government was considered nearly obsolete and available only in older firearms. Based on a lengthened .44 Magnum case, the .444 Marlin pushed .44-caliber rifle bullets to velocities significantly higher than the revolver cartridge, launching 240-grain bullets at approximately 2,400 fps from the Marlin 336-based Model 444 lever-action rifle.

Early ammunition used repurposed .44-caliber pistol bullets that were not designed for the higher velocities and rifle-barrel twist rates, leading to accuracy issues and inadequate terminal performance at range. The cartridge's reputation suffered until Hornady and other manufacturers developed proper .429-caliber rifle bullets in heavier weights of 265 to 300 grains, with jackets designed for rifle velocities. These improved bullets transformed the .444 Marlin from a problematic curiosity into a genuinely effective big-game cartridge. The development of Hornady's LEVERevolution ammunition with polymer-tipped bullets further improved the cartridge's ballistic performance in tubular magazines.

## Modern Usage

The .444 Marlin serves as a potent brush-country and timber hunting cartridge, particularly effective on large game at ranges inside 200 yards. With modern 265 to 300-grain bullets, it delivers tremendous energy on target, making it an excellent choice for elk, moose, black bear, and wild boar. The heavy, large-diameter bullets penetrate deeply and create significant wound channels, and the cartridge is a popular choice among hunters in the thick forests of the eastern United States and Canada where long shots are rare.

While the resurgence of the .45-70 Government has overshadowed the .444 Marlin in recent years, the two cartridges serve overlapping but slightly different roles. The .444 Marlin typically operates at higher pressures and can drive its bullets to faster velocities, giving it a flatter trajectory advantage over standard-pressure .45-70 loads. Factory ammunition is available from Hornady, Federal, and others, though selection is more limited than the .45-70. The primary platform remains the Marlin 444 lever-action, and the cartridge has a devoted following among hunters who appreciate its combination of lever-action handling and big-bore stopping power in a package that is compact and fast-pointing in dense cover.
