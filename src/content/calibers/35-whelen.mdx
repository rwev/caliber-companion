## History

The .35 Whelen originated around 1922 as a wildcat cartridge, the product of collaboration between Colonel Townsend Whelen, one of America's most respected firearms authorities, and gunsmith James Howe of Griffin & Howe. The concept was elegantly simple: neck up the ubiquitous .30-06 Springfield case to accept .35-caliber bullets, creating a more powerful cartridge that could function in standard-length bolt actions. The resulting round pushed 250-grain bullets to approximately 2,400 fps, offering a significant step up in energy for hunters pursuing elk, moose, and large bear.

For over six decades, the .35 Whelen existed exclusively as a wildcat, gaining a dedicated following among handloaders and custom rifle enthusiasts. Remington finally standardized and commercially adopted the cartridge in 1988, chambering it in their Model 700 Classic. This legitimized the round and made factory ammunition available, though the cartridge never achieved mainstream popularity. The .35 Whelen Improved variant, with less body taper and a sharper shoulder, offered modest velocity gains and became popular among wildcatters seeking to maximize the cartridge's potential.

## Modern Usage

The .35 Whelen remains a favorite among knowledgeable big game hunters who appreciate its ability to deliver heavy, large-diameter bullets at velocities that ensure reliable expansion and deep penetration. With 225 to 250-grain premium bullets, it is an outstanding cartridge for elk and moose at ranges inside 300 yards, where its large frontal area and high sectional density projectiles create decisive wound channels. Many Alaskan guides and experienced hunters consider it one of the best all-around choices for mixed bag hunts where one might encounter anything from deer to brown bear.

Factory ammunition is produced by Federal, Remington, and several smaller manufacturers, though the selection is limited compared to more popular cartridges. The .35 Whelen's niche status works in its favor among enthusiasts who value it precisely because it is overlooked by the mainstream market. The cartridge's .30-06 parentage means that any standard-length action can be easily rebarreled, and brass is readily formed from .30-06 cases, making it an excellent choice for handloaders. While it will likely never rival the commercial success of the .30-06 or .308, the .35 Whelen endures as a practical and effective big-bore option for serious hunters.
