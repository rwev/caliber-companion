## History

The 7.5×55mm Swiss cartridge traces its origins to 1889, when Switzerland adopted its first smokeless-powder military cartridge alongside the Schmidt-Rubin Model 1889 rifle. The original loading used a 211-grain round-nose projectile, but the cartridge was significantly modernized in 1911 with the adoption of the GP 11 (Gewehrpatrone 11) loading — a 174-grain boat-tail spitzer bullet loaded to exacting Swiss manufacturing standards. The GP 11 became one of the most accurate military ball cartridges ever produced, a distinction it held for decades.

The cartridge served in a succession of Swiss military rifles, but its most famous pairing is with the K31 (Karabiner Model 1931), a straight-pull bolt-action rifle of remarkable precision. Swiss soldiers were expected to maintain high standards of marksmanship, and the combination of the accurate K31 action with the inherently consistent GP 11 ammunition made the Swiss militia one of the most skilled rifleman forces in Europe. The cartridge later served in the SIG SG 510 (Stgw 57) semi-automatic battle rifle before Switzerland transitioned to the 5.56×45mm NATO with the SIG SG 550 in the 1990s.

## Modern Usage

The 7.5×55mm Swiss occupies a devoted niche among surplus rifle enthusiasts and precision shooters. The K31 is widely regarded as one of the finest military bolt-action rifles ever produced, and examples imported to the civilian market have developed a cult following for their remarkable accuracy — many will produce sub-MOA groups with GP 11 surplus ammunition straight from the crate. Swiss surplus GP 11 ammunition, produced at the government RUAG facility, is prized for its consistency and precision, though supplies have diminished in recent years. Commercial ammunition from PPU and RUAG remains available, and handloaders can achieve outstanding results with quality components. The cartridge delivers ballistics comparable to the .308 Winchester, making it fully capable as a hunting round for medium to large game.
