## History

The .416 Rigby was designed by John Rigby & Company of London in 1911, created to provide professional hunters and ivory hunters in Africa with a cartridge capable of reliably stopping elephant, Cape buffalo, and other dangerous game. The cartridge was built around the large Magnum Mauser action, using an oversized case with a 0.586-inch rim diameter that operated at relatively low chamber pressures of around 47,000 psi. This conservative pressure approach was deliberate — in the extreme heat of equatorial Africa, where ambient temperatures could cause dangerous pressure spikes in tightly loaded cartridges, the .416 Rigby's generous case capacity provided a crucial safety margin.

The cartridge earned legendary status in the hands of professional hunters like Harry Selby, who used it extensively during decades of safari work in Botswana and East Africa. Its 400-grain bullets at 2,400 fps offered a combination of penetration and stopping power that few cartridges could match. Production of both rifles and ammunition declined significantly during and after World War II, and for decades the .416 Rigby was nearly extinct. The cartridge experienced a dramatic revival in the 1980s and 1990s when CZ began producing affordable magnum-action rifles and Hornady, Federal, and others reintroduced factory ammunition.

## Modern Usage

Today the .416 Rigby enjoys renewed respect as one of the premier dangerous game cartridges. It is widely regarded as one of the finest choices for Cape buffalo hunting, where its 400-grain bullets deliver exceptional penetration through heavy bone and muscle. The cartridge's relatively low operating pressure continues to be an advantage in tropical conditions, and its large powder column provides consistent ignition even with temperature-sensitive propellants.

Modern rifle offerings include the CZ 550 Safari Magnum, which brought the .416 Rigby within financial reach of many more hunters, as well as premium rifles from the revived Rigby company, Dakota Arms, and various custom Mauser 98 builders. Ammunition from Hornady, Federal, and Norma is readily available in both expanding and solid configurations suitable for dangerous game work. The .416 Rigby requires a magnum-length action, resulting in heavier rifles that help mitigate its substantial recoil. While not a high-volume seller, its reputation as perhaps the finest all-around dangerous game cartridge ensures continued demand from serious safari hunters worldwide.
