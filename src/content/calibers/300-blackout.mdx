## History

The .300 AAC Blackout was developed by Advanced Armament Corporation (AAC) in cooperation with Remington Defense, with the cartridge receiving SAAMI acceptance in 2011. The project was driven by a U.S. military requirement for a .30-caliber cartridge that could be used in the M4 carbine platform with only a barrel change — retaining the same bolt, magazines, and lower receiver. AAC engineer Robert Silvers led the design effort, building on earlier wildcat cartridges like J.D. Jones' .300 Whisper. The key innovation was optimizing the cartridge for both supersonic and subsonic performance from short barrels while maintaining reliable cycling in semi-automatic and fully automatic weapons. The case was formed by necking up the 5.56x45mm case to accept .308-caliber bullets, ensuring magazine compatibility and identical bolt face dimensions.

The cartridge was specifically designed with suppressor use as a primary consideration, not an afterthought. Subsonic loads pushing 190-220 grain bullets at velocities below 1,050 fps produce remarkably quiet reports when suppressed, while supersonic loads with 110-125 grain bullets deliver ballistic performance comparable to the 7.62x39mm Soviet cartridge. This dual-personality capability set the .300 Blackout apart from every other cartridge in the AR-15 ecosystem.

## Modern Usage

The .300 Blackout has become one of the most popular AR-15 cartridges in America, second only to the 5.56x45mm itself. Its rise has closely tracked the explosive growth of suppressor ownership in the civilian market following the increased availability of NFA items. Home defense is a major use case, where the combination of .30-caliber projectiles, moderate velocity, and suppressor compatibility offers advantages over the 5.56mm in certain scenarios. Hunters have adopted the cartridge for hog hunting and deer-sized game at moderate ranges, particularly from compact, suppressed platforms. U.S. special operations forces employ the cartridge in suppressed roles where the reduced sound signature of subsonic loads provides a tactical advantage. The ammunition market is mature, with factory options ranging from inexpensive supersonic plinking loads to premium subsonic hunting and defensive ammunition from all major manufacturers.
