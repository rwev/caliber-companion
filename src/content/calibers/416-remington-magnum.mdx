## History

The .416 Remington Magnum was introduced in 1988, born from the resurgence of interest in .416-caliber dangerous game cartridges during the 1980s. With the .416 Rigby experiencing a revival but requiring expensive magnum-length actions, Remington saw an opportunity to offer equivalent performance in their standard .375 H&H-length action. The design was straightforward: a full-length belted magnum case necked up to accept .416-caliber 400-grain bullets, achieving 2,400 fps muzzle velocity — matching the classic .416 Rigby specification.

The key advantage was practical. Where the .416 Rigby demanded a costly magnum Mauser or similarly oversized action, the .416 Remington Magnum fit into any rifle built for the .375 H&H family of cartridges, including Remington's own Model 700. This dramatically lowered the cost of entry into serious dangerous game rifle ownership. The tradeoff was higher chamber pressure — approximately 62,000 psi compared to the Rigby's 47,000 psi — which some hunters viewed as a disadvantage in extreme tropical heat. Despite this concern, the cartridge proved reliable and effective in field use across Africa and elsewhere.

## Modern Usage

The .416 Remington Magnum serves as a practical dangerous game cartridge for hunters who want .416-caliber performance without the expense and bulk of a magnum-length action rifle. It meets the legal minimum requirements for dangerous game in virtually all African countries and delivers proven terminal performance on Cape buffalo, hippopotamus, and other heavy, dangerous species. The 400-grain bullets at 2,400 fps provide the deep, straight-line penetration that professional hunters demand.

Rifles chambered in .416 Remington Magnum are available from several manufacturers, including the Winchester Model 70 Safari Express and CZ 550. Ammunition is produced by Federal, Hornady, Norma, and others in both expanding and solid configurations. While the cartridge generates punishing recoil that demands proper shooting technique and a well-fitted rifle, experienced dangerous game hunters appreciate its balance of power, availability, and platform compatibility. It remains a sound choice for any hunter building a dangerous game rifle on a standard magnum action.
