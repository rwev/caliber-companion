## History

The .25-06 Remington is another cartridge with deep wildcat roots. Experimenters began necking down the .30-06 Springfield case to .25 caliber almost as soon as the parent cartridge was available, with noted firearms writer A.O. Niedner popularizing the concept in the 1920s under the name .25 Niedner. The wildcat offered remarkable velocity from the large .30-06 case — light .25-caliber bullets could be driven well past 3,400 fps — but early powders were not ideally suited to the combination's large case capacity and small bore diameter, leading to inconsistent performance and accelerated barrel erosion.

The development of slower-burning progressive propellants in the postwar era solved many of these issues, and the .25-06 wildcat grew steadily in popularity through the 1950s and 1960s. Remington standardized the cartridge in 1969, introducing it in their Model 700 bolt-action rifle. By this time, the round had over four decades of proven performance in the field, and factory legitimization was welcomed by handloaders who wanted the convenience of commercially available brass and loaded ammunition. The .25-06 quickly established itself as a premier long-range deer and antelope cartridge.

## Modern Usage

The .25-06 Remington occupies an interesting niche as one of the flattest-shooting non-magnum cartridges available. With 100-grain bullets at roughly 3,200 fps, it produces a trajectory that rivals many belted magnums without the punishing recoil, making it a natural choice for hunters pursuing pronghorn, mule deer, and whitetails in open country where long shots are the norm. Its versatility extends to the varmint field, where 85-grain bullets at 3,400 fps deliver impressive performance on coyotes and similar-sized predators. The cartridge does require a standard-length action, which makes for a slightly longer and heavier rifle than short-action alternatives like the .257 Roberts Improved, but most hunters consider this a negligible trade-off. While the .25-06 has never achieved the mass popularity of the .270 Winchester or .30-06 Springfield, it maintains a dedicated following among hunters who appreciate its unique combination of speed, reach, and moderate recoil.
