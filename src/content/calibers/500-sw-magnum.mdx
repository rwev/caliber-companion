## History

The .500 Smith & Wesson Magnum was developed jointly by Smith & Wesson and Cor-Bon ammunition, introduced in February 2003 alongside the massive Smith & Wesson Model 500 revolver built on the new X-Frame platform. The project was driven by a desire to create the most powerful production handgun cartridge in the world, reclaiming a title that had passed through the .454 Casull and .475 Linebaugh.

The cartridge uses a half-inch diameter bullet in a case 1.625 inches long, generating chamber pressures of 60,000 psi. With a 400-grain bullet at over 1,650 fps, or a 275-grain bullet at over 2,000 fps, the .500 S&W produces energy levels approaching those of the .30-30 Winchester rifle cartridge — from a handgun. The Model 500 revolver required an entirely new frame size, the X-Frame, as no existing revolver platform could handle the cartridge's extreme pressures and recoil forces.

## Modern Usage

The .500 S&W Magnum occupies the extreme end of the handgun spectrum. Its primary practical applications are handgun hunting for large and dangerous game, and as an ultimate bear defense cartridge in wilderness areas. Guides in Alaska and northern Canada sometimes carry the Model 500 as protection against brown bears and polar bears, where its massive energy delivery provides a decisive margin of safety.

The cartridge has also developed a following among big-bore sport shooters who enjoy the challenge and spectacle of managing extreme recoil. Ammunition options range from relatively mild 275-grain loads to earth-shaking 500-grain penetrators. Big Horn Armory produces a lever-action rifle chambered in .500 S&W Magnum, which tames the recoil while extracting even higher velocities. While the .500 S&W Magnum will never be a mainstream cartridge, it represents the absolute pinnacle of handgun power and continues to fascinate firearms enthusiasts worldwide.
