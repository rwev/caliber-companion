## History

The .260 Remington was standardized by Remington in 1997, though the concept of necking down the .308 Winchester case to 6.5mm had existed in wildcat form as the 6.5-08 A-Square for years before. The idea was simple and compelling: take the proven .308 Winchester case and mate it with the inherently efficient, high-BC 6.5mm (.264-inch) bullets that European cartridge designers had appreciated for a century. The result was a cartridge that could launch 140-grain 6.5mm bullets at competitive velocities from a short-action rifle while generating significantly less recoil than the parent .308 Winchester.

The .260 Remington gained a strong following among competitive shooters and precision rifle enthusiasts through the early 2000s. In NRA High Power competition, it became a dominant force, with multiple national champions using the cartridge. Early adopters in the tactical precision rifle community recognized its advantages in reduced recoil and superior wind-bucking ability compared to the .308 Winchester. However, Remington's marketing support was tepid, and factory rifle and ammunition options were limited compared to the .308. This opened the door for the 6.5 Creedmoor, introduced in 2007 with better commercial support, which eventually eclipsed the .260 Remington in market share.

## Modern Usage

Despite living in the shadow of the 6.5 Creedmoor, the .260 Remington retains a dedicated following, particularly among handloaders who appreciate the slightly larger case capacity that yields a modest velocity advantage of 50 to 100 fps with the same bullets. Ballistically, the two cartridges are near-twins, and the .260 Remington can do anything the 6.5 Creedmoor can do. For hunters, the cartridge is an excellent choice for deer, antelope, and similar medium game, with enough energy for elk at moderate ranges when loaded with premium 140-grain bonded or monolithic bullets.

In the precision rifle world, the .260 Remington continues to appear at Precision Rifle Series matches and other competitive venues, though the 6.5 Creedmoor has become the more common choice simply due to wider factory ammunition availability. The .260 Remington's greatest strength in the modern era is as a handloader's cartridge — its .308 Winchester parentage means brass is easily formed, and the slightly longer neck compared to the 6.5 Creedmoor is favored by some for bullet concentricity and consistent neck tension. Factory ammunition is available from Federal, Hornady, Nosler, and others, ensuring the cartridge remains accessible even to those who do not reload.
